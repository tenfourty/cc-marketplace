## Overview
The Energy Audit is a structured inventory of recurring activities categorized by their effect on personal energy: gives energy (+), drains energy (-), or neutral (=). By mapping time spent against energy return, patterns emerge that reveal where energy is being silently consumed. The output is a concrete restructuring plan — delegating, reducing, or eliminating draining activities while protecting and expanding energizing ones.

## When to Use
- User reports feeling exhausted or burned out without a clear cause
- User says "I'm always busy but never feel like I'm doing the right things"
- User wants to protect their energy as they scale responsibilities
- After a Wheel of Life or PERMA assessment reveals low energy, engagement, or fun
- User is returning from a break and wants to reset their workload intentionally
- User is in a role transition and designing what they want their weeks to look like

## When NOT to Use
- User's energy issues are primarily physical (sleep disorder, illness, nutrition) — redirect to health resources first
- User is in a crisis or survival mode where there is no discretion over activities
- User has very limited control over their schedule (highly constrained role) — the audit is still useful for awareness, but temper restructuring expectations
- User has already done this recently (last 4–8 weeks) and the situation hasn't changed

## The Assessment

Run this as a structured conversation over 2–3 exchanges. The goal is a complete, honest inventory — not a curated one.

### Step 1: Brain Dump All Recurring Activities

Ask the user to list every recurring activity they do — work and personal. Prompt for completeness:

- What does a typical Monday through Friday look like, hour by hour?
- What meetings or calls do you attend regularly?
- What types of work tasks fill most of your time?
- What admin, logistics, or operational tasks recur?
- What social or family obligations recur?
- What do you do to recover — exercise, hobbies, routines?
- What are the unplanned but recurring interruptions that eat your time?

Capture everything. Common categories to prompt for if missing:
- Email / Slack / comms management
- 1:1 meetings (each one is its own entry)
- Status meetings / standups
- Strategic thinking / planning
- Deep work / focused creation
- Hiring / interviewing
- Mentoring or coaching others
- Presentations / stakeholder communication
- Conflict resolution / firefighting
- Administrative tasks (expenses, approvals, scheduling)
- Learning / reading / research
- Physical health (workouts, walks)
- Family time, social commitments
- Personal routines (morning, evening)

### Step 2: Categorize Each Activity

For each activity, ask the user: "When you do this, does it give you energy, drain you, or feel neutral?"

Label each:
- **(+) Gives energy:** After doing this, you feel more alive, engaged, or motivated. You'd do it even if it weren't required.
- **(-) Drains energy:** After doing this, you feel depleted, resentful, or flat. You dread it or need to recover afterward.
- **(=) Neutral:** It doesn't move the needle either way. Fine to do, fine to skip.

### Step 3: Estimate Time Spent Per Week

For each activity, estimate hours per week (or hours per occurrence × frequency). Round to nearest 0.5h. Total should roughly equal a typical week.

### Step 4: Summarize the Energy Budget

Calculate:
- Total hours on (+) activities: ___h
- Total hours on (-) activities: ___h
- Total hours on (=) activities: ___h
- % of waking/working hours on (+): ___%
- % of waking/working hours on (-): ___%

A rough heuristic from Mochary: aim for 75%+ of working time on (+) activities. Below 50% is a strong signal for restructuring.

### Step 5: Design the Restructuring Plan

For each (-) activity, explore one of four options:
1. **Eliminate:** Can it stop happening entirely?
2. **Delegate:** Can someone else own it?
3. **Systematize:** Can a process, tool, or template reduce the cognitive load?
4. **Reframe:** Is there a way to do this that makes it energizing? (Rare, but sometimes applicable)

For each (+) activity, ask:
- Is this protected on the calendar?
- Could you do more of it?
- Is anything unnecessarily blocking access to it?

## Interpretation Guide

**High (-) time (>40% of work hours on draining activities):**
The user is running on willpower, not intrinsic energy. Burnout is likely or approaching. Prioritize delegation and elimination before anything else.

**Low (+) time (<30% on energizing activities):**
Even if total hours are reasonable, chronic under-exposure to energizing work leads to flatness and disengagement. Protect and expand (+) time first.

**Many (-) activities are people-based (certain meetings, certain relationships):**
Energy drain from specific people is often an interpersonal dynamics issue. This warrants a separate coaching conversation.

**Most (-) activities are structural (admin, reporting, coordination):**
A process or delegation problem. The fix is systemic — tools, hiring, or role redefinition.

**All activities feel neutral:**
Often a sign of disengagement from the work itself. Probe whether any activity used to be energizing and what changed.

**Discrepancy between what is "important" and what is energizing:**
Some draining activities may be necessary (strategic conversations the user dreads, for example). The goal is not to eliminate discomfort entirely — it's to ensure the energy account is in surplus.

## Follow-Up Questions
- Which (-) activity surprised you most when you named it?
- Is there a (-) activity you've been tolerating for years? What has it cost you?
- What does your calendar look like compared to this audit — does it reflect your intentions?
- If you could only protect one (+) activity this week, which would you choose?
- Who in your life or team could take on one of your (-) activities?
- What would need to change organizationally for your (+) percentage to double?
- Looking at your neutral activities — are any of them "fine" habits hiding the cost of not doing something better?

## Pitfalls
- Users often conflate "important" with "energizing" — keep them separate. Important things can still drain energy.
- Watch for guilt around (-) labeling — users may resist calling a meeting with a valued colleague draining. Normalize it.
- The audit captures a moment in time — activities can shift between categories under stress or in different life phases
- Don't rush to restructuring before the inventory is complete; an incomplete audit produces a partial plan
- Some draining activities genuinely can't be eliminated or delegated yet — acknowledge this and work with what is moveable
- Restructuring requires a second conversation or session; don't try to solve everything in the audit session itself
