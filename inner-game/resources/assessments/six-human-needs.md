## Overview
The Six Human Needs framework, developed by Tony Robbins and Cloe Madanes, proposes that all human behavior is driven by six universal needs: Certainty, Variety, Significance, Connection/Love, Growth, and Contribution. The first four are needs of the personality; the last two are needs of the spirit. Everyone has all six, but each person prioritizes them differently. Your top two needs function as primary drivers — understanding them explains recurring choices, conflicts, and patterns that otherwise look irrational.

## When to Use
- User is confused about why they keep making the same choices or ending up in the same situations
- User has a recurring pattern they can't break (overworking, sabotaging relationships, seeking validation, avoiding commitment)
- User experiences intense emotional reactions to specific situations (e.g., being micromanaged, being overlooked, being too comfortable)
- Relationship or team conflict where parties seem to fundamentally disagree about what matters
- User wants to understand what actually drives their behavior at a deeper level
- User is making a major decision and wants to understand what needs it is serving

## When NOT to Use
- User wants a practical inventory of their life (use Wheel of Life or Energy Audit first)
- User is not ready for introspective work — this framework requires honest self-examination
- User just wants validation, not insight — the framework will surface uncomfortable truths
- Use cautiously if user is in active crisis; stabilize first

## The Assessment

Administer this in two phases: a scored questionnaire to surface the top needs, followed by behavioral validation to confirm them.

### Phase 1: Scored Questionnaire

Ask the user to score each statement from 1 to 6.
1 = not like me at all, 6 = this is very much me.

**Certainty (Security, Comfort, Predictability)**
C1. I prefer to know what's coming — surprises make me uncomfortable. (1–6)
C2. I work hard to make sure outcomes are within my control. (1–6)
C3. I feel anxious or unsettled when things are unstable or unpredictable. (1–6)
C4. I am most productive when I have a clear routine or structure. (1–6)

Certainty Score: ___ (sum of C1–C4, max 24)

**Variety (Uncertainty, Change, Stimulation)**
V1. I get bored quickly and seek new challenges, experiences, or perspectives. (1–6)
V2. I thrive under pressure or in fast-changing environments. (1–6)
V3. Routine feels like a trap — I need novelty to feel alive. (1–6)
V4. I often create drama or chaos, even unconsciously, when things feel too settled. (1–6)

Variety Score: ___ (sum of V1–V4, max 24)

**Significance (Importance, Recognition, Uniqueness)**
S1. I need to feel that I matter — that my contributions are noticed and valued. (1–6)
S2. Being seen as competent, successful, or unique is important to me. (1–6)
S3. I work harder when I know my efforts will be recognized. (1–6)
S4. I feel diminished when I'm treated as ordinary or interchangeable. (1–6)

Significance Score: ___ (sum of S1–S4, max 24)

**Connection/Love (Bonding, Belonging, Intimacy)**
L1. Deep, genuine connection with others is one of the most important things in my life. (1–6)
L2. I often sacrifice my own needs to preserve relationships or be liked. (1–6)
L3. Being alone for extended periods leaves me feeling depleted or anxious. (1–6)
L4. I work harder or perform better when I feel close to the people I'm working with. (1–6)

Connection Score: ___ (sum of L1–L4, max 24)

**Growth (Learning, Development, Expansion)**
G1. I am consistently driven to improve — at work, in relationships, in myself. (1–6)
G2. Stagnation or repetition feels deeply uncomfortable to me. (1–6)
G3. I invest heavily in learning, development, and becoming more capable. (1–6)
G4. A life without growth feels like a life being wasted. (1–6)

Growth Score: ___ (sum of G1–G4, max 24)

**Contribution (Giving, Service, Legacy)**
Co1. My deepest satisfaction comes from making a difference beyond myself. (1–6)
Co2. I am drawn to roles, projects, or causes where my work helps others. (1–6)
Co3. I feel most alive when I know my efforts will outlast me — leaving something behind. (1–6)
Co4. I find it hard to stay motivated if the work only benefits me personally. (1–6)

Contribution Score: ___ (sum of Co1–Co4, max 24)

**Ranking:** List all six needs by score, highest to lowest. The top two are the primary drivers.

### Phase 2: Behavioral Validation

For the top two needs identified, run through these confirming questions:

- "Can you think of a time when this need was unmet? What did you do?"
- "Can you think of a behavior pattern you've noticed in yourself that this need might explain?"
- "When this need is met, how do you feel? When it's threatened, what happens?"

Adjust the ranking if behavioral evidence contradicts the scores — behavior is the ground truth.

## Interpretation Guide

**Needs of Personality (top four needs — everyone meets these somehow):**
- **Certainty + Variety in conflict:** A person who needs both will swing between seeking security and blowing it up. Common pattern in serial entrepreneurs, relationship-avoiders, and change-junkies who hate change happening to them.
- **Significance + Connection in conflict:** Significance seeks to stand out; Connection seeks to belong. A person driven by both may crave intimacy but self-sabotage it by needing to be the most important person in the room.
- **High Certainty:** May manifest as control, rigidity, over-preparation, or avoidance of risk. Not pathological — but can limit growth.
- **High Variety:** May manifest as impulsivity, novelty-seeking, self-sabotage of stable situations, or manufactured drama. The vehicle for meeting Variety can be healthy (travel, learning, creative work) or destructive (conflict, affairs, recklessness).
- **High Significance:** May manifest as overworking, competition, status-seeking, being contrarian, or making others feel small. Often co-exists with deep insecurity.
- **High Connection:** May manifest as people-pleasing, difficulty with boundaries, fear of abandonment, or subordinating needs to relationships.

**Needs of Spirit (Growth and Contribution — the two that produce lasting fulfillment):**
- A life organized around only the first four needs tends to feel hollow over time, even when those needs are met. Growth and Contribution produce a deeper, more durable sense of meaning.
- If Growth or Contribution score high but aren't being met, the user often describes a sense of "something missing" even when life looks good on paper.
- If neither Growth nor Contribution registers strongly, explore whether they've been suppressed (too much survival mode) or whether they genuinely haven't activated yet.

**Meeting needs through destructive vehicles:**
Every need can be met in healthy or unhealthy ways. A person meeting Significance through victimhood, Certainty through control of others, or Variety through crisis is still meeting a real need — the vehicle is the problem, not the need itself. Surfacing the underlying need is the first step to finding a better vehicle.

## Follow-Up Questions
- Does seeing your top two needs explain any patterns in your life that previously didn't make sense?
- How are you currently meeting your top need — is the vehicle serving you?
- Can you think of a conflict in your life that might be a clash between your needs and someone else's top needs?
- What would it look like to meet your [top need] in a more intentional, healthier way?
- If you designed your ideal role / relationship / life with your top two needs in mind, what would change?
- Are you currently meeting your Growth or Contribution needs? What happens when you don't?
- What need do you tend to neglect? What is that costing you?

## Pitfalls
- Scores are a starting point — always validate with behavioral evidence. Self-report bias is significant here.
- Avoid pathologizing any need; all six are universal and healthy. The framework is about prioritization and vehicles, not diagnosis.
- High Significance often generates resistance when named — users may feel judged. Frame it as a strength with a shadow, not a flaw.
- Connection and Significance are often confused — a person might score both high; probe which one they'd sacrifice if forced to choose.
- The framework explains behavior, not destiny — use it to open possibility, not to label or limit the user.
- Don't rush to reframe destructive vehicles; the user needs to first see and accept the need before they can change how they meet it.
