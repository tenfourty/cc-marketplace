## Overview
MI is a collaborative conversation style for strengthening a person's own motivation and commitment to change. Developed in clinical psychology but widely adapted for coaching, leadership, and behaviour change. The core insight: arguing for change creates resistance; evoking the person's own reasons for change creates movement. The coach's job is to draw out "change talk" — the person's own arguments for changing.

## When to Use
- User is ambivalent — they express wanting to change but also reasons not to
- User is resistant, defensive, or seems to be going through the motions
- User has tried and failed at something repeatedly and is discouraged
- Behaviour change goals (habits, health, routines, communication patterns)
- User is saying "yes, but..." to every option
- Readiness to change is the issue, not knowledge or skill

## When NOT to Use
- User is already motivated and ready — MI slows things down unnecessarily (use GROW instead)
- User needs skill-building or information, not motivational work
- **Clinical note**: Full MI is a therapeutic modality. This adapted version is for coaching. If the user describes addiction, eating disorders, or other clinical presentations, refer them to a qualified professional. Do not attempt clinical MI.

## The Framework

### The Spirit of MI (non-negotiable foundation)
MI rests on four relational qualities — without these, the techniques are manipulative:
- **Partnership**: Collaboration, not expert-to-patient. The coach is a companion, not a director.
- **Acceptance**: Unconditional positive regard, accurate empathy, autonomy support. Meet the person where they are.
- **Compassion**: Actively promote the person's wellbeing. Their agenda, not yours.
- **Evocation**: The resources and motivation for change are within the person. Draw them out, don't install them.

### OARS — Core Techniques

**Open Questions**
Questions that invite elaboration, not yes/no answers. "What brings you here?" not "Are you ready to change?" Open questions are the primary vehicle for evoking change talk.

**Affirmations**
Genuine statements that acknowledge strengths, efforts, and values — not hollow praise. "You've tried this several times despite setbacks — that persistence matters." Affirmations build the self-efficacy needed for change.

**Reflections**
Repeating or paraphrasing what the person said (simple reflection) or going beneath the surface to name feelings, meanings, or implications (complex reflection). Reflections communicate understanding and reduce defensiveness. When someone feels heard, resistance drops.

**Summaries**
Periodically gather and offer back what has been said — particularly the change talk. Summaries create coherence, signal active listening, and allow the person to hear their own words about change.

### Change Talk vs Sustain Talk
**Change talk** = the person's own arguments for change. DARN-CAT:
- **D**esire: "I want to..."
- **A**bility: "I could..."
- **R**easons: "It would be better if..."
- **N**eed: "I have to..."
- **C**ommitment: "I will..."
- **A**ctivation: "I'm ready to..."
- **T**aking steps: "I've started..."

**Sustain talk** = the person's arguments for not changing. Normal and expected. Do not argue against it; acknowledge it and return attention to change talk.

**Rolling with resistance**: When someone pushes back, don't push back. Reflect it, name it without judgment, and continue exploring. "It sounds like part of you really doesn't want to change this." Resistance is information, not an obstacle to overcome.

### Readiness Rulers
A simple evocative tool: "On a scale of 0–10, how important is this change to you?" Then: "Why a [X] and not a [X-2]?" This surfaces the person's own reasons for caring — which are more powerful than reasons the coach provides.

Follow with: "And on a scale of 0–10, how confident are you that you could make this change if you decided to?" The gap between importance and confidence is diagnostic.

## Key Questions

**Opening and exploring ambivalence:**
- "What brings this up for you now?"
- "What would you like to see different?"
- "What are the good things about [current situation]? And what are the less good things?"
- "What concerns you about making this change?"

**Evoking change talk:**
- "Why would you want to make this change?"
- "If you did make this change, what would be the best possible outcome?"
- "What would your life look like in 5 years if you made this change — and if you didn't?"
- "What's the most important reason you can think of to make this change?"

**Addressing ambivalence:**
- "It sounds like part of you wants to change, and part of you isn't sure. Tell me more about both sides."
- "What would have to be different for you to feel more ready?"

**Readiness rulers:**
- "On a scale of 0–10, how important is this change to you right now?"
- "Why [X] and not [X-2]?"
- "What would it take to move from [X] to [X+2]?"

## Pitfalls
- **The righting reflex**: The urge to fix, advise, or convince. If the coach is arguing for change, the person argues against it. Stop talking and ask a question.
- **Arguing with sustain talk**: Directly countering "I can't do that" with "Yes you can!" entrenches resistance. Reflect and redirect.
- **Premature focus on solutions**: Jumping to action before motivation is established. The person needs to articulate their own reasons first.
- **Formulaic OARS**: Mechanically cycling through Open Question → Reflection → Affirmation is detectable and feels hollow. OARS is a vocabulary, not a script.
- **Confusing this with therapy**: MI adapted for coaching focuses on motivation and behaviour change goals, not psychological disorders. Know the boundary and hold it.
