## Overview
MBS identified 7 questions that, when used consistently, break the habit of over-advice-giving and create real coaching conversations. The questions are deliberately simple — the power is in asking and then staying quiet. The AWE question ("And what else?") is the single most useful tool in this set: it buys time, generates options, and prevents the coach from jumping to solutions.

## When to Use
- Any coaching conversation — these questions work as a standalone structure or woven into other frameworks
- When the coach catches themselves about to give advice or solve the problem
- When the user brings too many topics at once (Kickstart question)
- When the conversation loses momentum or goes shallow (AWE question)
- When wrapping up any coaching conversation (Learning question)

## When NOT to Use
- User is in crisis — these questions assume psychological safety and basic regulation
- User has explicitly asked for information, expertise, or advice (give it, then check in)
- The session is structured around a different framework with its own question set — borrow individual questions rather than using all 7

## The Framework

The 7 questions follow a rough arc: open the conversation, go deeper, find the real issue, clarify the want, define the help needed, create boundaries, and close with learning. They do not need to be used in strict sequence — the AWE question can follow any of the others.

### 1. The Kickstart Question
**"What's on your mind?"**
Opens without constraining. Invites the real topic rather than the presenting topic. Better than "How are you?" (too vague) or "How did X go?" (too narrow).

### 2. The AWE Question
**"And what else?"**
The workhorse. Use it after almost any answer. Generates more options, prevents premature closure, signals the coach is not rushing to solve. Ask it 3–4 times until the answer is genuinely "nothing else." Then stop.

### 3. The Focus Question
**"What's the real challenge here for you?"**
Three words matter: "real" (cuts to what actually matters), "challenge" (problem-focused but not blame-focused), "for you" (makes it personal, not abstract). Prevents the user from coaching about someone else's problem.

### 4. The Foundation Question
**"What do you want?"**
Deceptively hard to answer. Distinguishes between wants and needs (Rosenberg's NVC is useful here). Often reveals that the stated problem is not the real one. Ask it when the conversation is going in circles.

### 5. The Lazy Question
**"How can I help?"**
Forces the user to make an explicit request rather than assuming the coach knows what they need. Prevents the coach from assuming and over-delivering. The word "lazy" is ironic — this takes discipline to ask.

### 6. The Strategic Question
**"If you're saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?"**
Creates clarity about trade-offs, opportunity costs, and commitment. Especially powerful for prioritisation decisions and overcommitment. Works equally well for projects, relationships, and time allocation.

### 7. The Learning Question
**"What was most useful for you?"**
Closes every conversation. Creates a habit of reflection in the user. Surfaces the value generated (useful for the coach). Ask it even when the session felt thin — the answer is often surprising.

**Supplementary probes that work well with these 7:**
- After Kickstart: "And what else is on your mind?" (AWE applied immediately)
- After Focus: "Is that the real challenge, or is there something deeper?"
- After Foundation: "What do you want, and what do you need — are they the same?"
- After Learning: "What will you do with that?"

## Pitfalls
- **Rushing past AWE**: Asking it once is not enough. The real answer is usually the third or fourth one.
- **Answering your own questions**: Asking "What's on your mind — is it the project thing?" collapses the question into advice. Ask and stop.
- **Skipping the Learning Question**: It feels like a formality. It is not. It is where the session lands.
- **Using the Lazy Question tentatively**: "I was wondering if maybe I could help with...?" is not the Lazy Question. Ask it flat: "How can I help?"
- **Focus Question without "for you"**: "What's the real challenge?" can stay abstract. "For you" makes it personal and breaks the pattern of coaching about third parties.
- **Using all 7 in sequence like a script**: These are moves, not a script. Read the conversation and deploy the right question at the right moment.
