## Overview
Tim Gallwey observed that elite athletes consistently underperform their own ability — not due to lack of skill but due to mental interference. He identified two "selves": Self 1, the conscious, analytical, judging mind that instructs and criticises; and Self 2, the body's natural intelligence that already knows how to perform. The Inner Game insight: most interference is Self 1 getting in the way of Self 2. Reducing interference, not adding more instruction, is the key to peak performance. Gallwey extended this beyond sports to work, learning, and life.

## When to Use
- User is over-thinking a task they are technically capable of doing
- Perfectionism, fear of failure, or self-criticism is the primary barrier — not skill
- User describes "performing below their level" in high-stakes situations (presentations, conversations, creative work)
- User is learning something new and internal criticism is slowing learning
- Imposter syndrome: user has the capability but does not trust themselves to use it
- The "trying harder" approach is making things worse — more effort is not the answer

## When NOT to Use
- The performance gap is genuinely a skill gap — Inner Game reduces interference; it does not build absent skills
- User needs strategic planning or decision-making, not performance coaching
- Emotional processing or values work is the primary need — ACT or co-active may be more appropriate
- User is not doing anything — they need activation, not interference reduction

## The Framework

### The Core Equation
**Performance = Potential − Interference**

This is not just a metaphor — it is a working model. Coaching interventions aimed at "building potential" often backfire because they add more Self 1 content (more instructions, more pressure, more should-statements). The high-leverage move is reducing interference: what is getting in the way of natural ability expressing itself?

### Self 1 vs Self 2

**Self 1** — the conscious, verbal, judging mind
- Instructs: "Keep your shoulders down. Don't forget to..."
- Evaluates: "That was terrible. Why can't you do this?"
- Predicts: "I'm going to mess this up"
- Controls: micromanages the body/attention moment to moment
Self 1 is not the enemy — it has a role in planning and learning. The problem is when it runs live performance.

**Self 2** — the body's natural intelligence and learned capability
- Contains all the competence built through practice and experience
- Operates best when trusted and not interfered with
- Learns naturally through observation, experience, and feedback — not through criticism
- Is the part of the person that already knows how to do this

The game is to quiet Self 1 enough that Self 2 can operate.

### The Three Core Skills

**1. Non-Judgmental Awareness (AWARENESS)**
The foundation. Observe what is actually happening without evaluation. "That shot went to the left" rather than "That was terrible." Data, not judgment. When Self 1 stops evaluating, the interference drops and natural correction happens.

Coaching application: Help the user describe their experience factually. "What do you notice is happening, without judging it?" Redirect evaluation to observation whenever it appears.

**2. Choice (CHOICE)**
Once interference is reduced, the person can make real choices — about attention, approach, method. Choice requires awareness first. Many people operate on autopilot or out of avoidance; genuine choice is only possible when the noise is quiet enough to hear what they actually want.

Coaching application: "Given what you've noticed, what do you *choose* to focus on?" Not "what should you do" — choice.

**3. Trust (TRUST)**
Trusting Self 2 — the natural, practiced capability. Letting go of micromanagement and allowing learned competence to operate. This is the hardest skill and is built through experiences of trusting and seeing that it works, not through reasoning about it.

Coaching application: "Can you try approaching this with a little more trust in what you already know? What would that look like?"

### The "Bounce-Hit" Principle (adapted)
Gallwey used "bounce-hit" in tennis to anchor attention on the ball (its bounce) and the racket contact (hit) — giving Self 1 something concrete to do that keeps it occupied and out of the way of Self 2's performance. The underlying principle for life coaching: give the judging mind a simple, specific focus task so it is not generating interference.

**Adapted for life coaching:**
When a user is overwhelmed by internal noise during a challenge, give Self 1 a single, neutral observation task: "In this conversation, just notice when the other person pauses." The specific focus absorbs Self 1 and allows natural social capability to operate.

### Self 1 Interference Patterns
Recognising how Self 1 shows up in coaching conversation:
- Constant self-evaluation during description of events ("and then I did this badly, and I messed up that part")
- Excessive instruction-giving to self: "I need to remember to X, and Y, and definitely Z"
- Suppression language: "I just need to stop feeling nervous"
- Identity-level judgment: "I'm just not a good [presenter / leader / communicator]"

Each of these is a coaching opportunity to redirect toward observation, not evaluation.

### Learning with Self 2
Self 2 learns most effectively through:
- **Observation**: seeing something done well and letting the nervous system absorb it
- **Feel and feedback**: trying something and noticing what happens (not evaluating what happened)
- **Curiosity**: "What would happen if I tried X?" rather than "I must get this right"

## Key Questions
- "What are you actually noticing is happening — without evaluating it yet?"
- "What's going on in your head while you're doing this?"
- "What does your inner critic sound like right now? What is it saying?"
- "If that critical voice went quiet for a moment, what would you naturally do?"
- "What do you already know how to do here that you're not letting yourself do?"
- "What would it look like to trust yourself a little more on this?"
- "You're trying very hard. What if trying less hard was the right move?"
- "What is the simplest thing you could focus on right now?"
- "Where is your attention going? Where do you *want* it to go?"
- "What gets in your way most — is it not knowing how, or not letting yourself do what you know?"

## Pitfalls
- **Adding more Self 1 instructions**: "You should be more aware of your thinking" is itself a Self 1 instruction. Coach through questions and curiosity, not directives.
- **Dismissing legitimate skill gaps**: Inner Game is not a substitute for skill development. If someone has never done the thing, they need experience, not just less self-criticism.
- **Pathologising Self 1**: Self 1 is not bad — it is useful for planning, learning new things, and safety. The problem is Self 1 running live performance. Don't create a new thing to judge ("I'm too in my head!").
- **Trust without competence**: Telling someone to "trust themselves" when they lack the underlying competence sets them up to fail. Build the skill first, then reduce interference.
- **One-conversation transformation**: Deeply ingrained self-critical patterns take time to shift. The coach's job is to give the user a direct experience of quieter Self 1 in the session — not to cure the pattern.
