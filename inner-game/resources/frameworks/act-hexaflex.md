## Overview
ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy/Training) builds psychological flexibility — the capacity to act effectively in the presence of difficult thoughts and feelings rather than being controlled by them. The hexaflex model describes six interconnected processes. Unlike CBT, ACT does not try to change the content of thoughts; it changes the *relationship* to thoughts and feelings. The goal is not to feel better, but to live better — in alignment with values, even when life is uncomfortable.

## When to Use
- User is avoiding important actions because of fear, anxiety, or discomfort ("I'd do it but...")
- User is caught in "experiential avoidance" — structuring life to avoid difficult feelings
- User feels stuck in overthinking / "analysis paralysis" and is fused with their thoughts
- Values are unclear or the user is living out of alignment with what matters to them
- User struggles with self-criticism, shame, or the inner critic
- Perfectionism, imposter syndrome, or fear of failure are the core issue
- User needs to make a committed decision and keep moving despite uncertainty

## When NOT to Use
- User needs clinical ACT (anxiety disorders, OCD, chronic pain, PTSD) — this is a coaching adaptation
- User wants concrete action planning without psychological process work — use GROW
- The issue is primarily skill-based or informational — don't introduce psychological flexibility when the gap is knowledge
- User is not open to experiential or values-based exploration

## The Framework

### The Hexaflex — 6 Processes of Psychological Flexibility

**1. Acceptance**
Willingness to have difficult thoughts and feelings without trying to control or eliminate them. Acceptance is *not* resignation or approval — it is making room for discomfort as a cost of doing what matters. The alternative (avoidance) typically makes the discomfort larger and limits life.

Coaching moves: "What would it look like to make room for that feeling rather than fighting it?" / "What has trying to avoid this feeling cost you?"

**2. Defusion (Cognitive Defusion)**
Separating from thoughts — seeing them as passing mental events rather than literal truths. A fused thought: "I am a failure." A defused version: "I notice I'm having the thought that I am a failure."

Techniques:
- Naming: "I'm having the thought that..." / "My mind is telling me..."
- Labelling the story: "There's the 'I'm not good enough' story again"
- Thanking the mind: "Thanks, mind. Noted." (takes the sting out)

The goal is not to believe the thought less — it is to hold it more lightly, so it no longer dictates behaviour.

**3. Present Moment Awareness**
Flexible, focused attention on the current experience rather than rumination (past) or worry (future). Not mindfulness as a relaxation technique, but as a behavioural tool — grounding attention in what is actually happening now, which is where action is possible.

Coaching moves: "What is actually happening right now, as opposed to what your mind is predicting?" / "Bring your attention to this conversation — what are you noticing?"

**4. Self-as-Context (The Observing Self)**
There is a perspective-taking "I" that observes all experiences — the observer is not defined by thoughts, feelings, roles, or stories. This is distinct from the "conceptualised self" (the story we tell about who we are). The observing self cannot be threatened by difficult thoughts, which is why it is a stable base for flexibility.

Coaching move: "You've noticed that thought — who is noticing?" / "There's the critical voice, and there's you watching it. What do you see from that observer position?"

**5. Values**
Chosen life directions — not goals (destinations you arrive at) but ongoing qualities of action. Values are intrinsic, not rules imposed from outside. They answer: "What do I want to stand for?" not "What should I want?"

Values clarification questions:
- "What matters most to you, deep down?"
- "If your actions perfectly reflected what you value, what would you be doing differently?"
- "When you imagine looking back at this period of your life, what do you want to have stood for?"

**6. Committed Action**
Taking effective action guided by values, even in the presence of difficult thoughts and feelings. Not "I'll act when I feel ready" but "I'll act, and I may feel uncomfortable doing it." Includes goal-setting, habits, and behavioural patterns — all anchored to values rather than mood.

Coaching move: "Given what you value, what is one action you could take this week — even if the anxiety is still there?"

### The ACT Triangle (core pattern to recognise)
Difficult event → Difficult thoughts/feelings → Avoidance/Fusion → Life constriction

The coaching intervention at each link:
- Difficult thoughts → Defusion
- Difficult feelings → Acceptance
- Avoidance → Values + Committed Action
- Rigid self-story → Self-as-Context

### Values vs Goals
Common confusion: "My value is to get promoted." That is a goal. The underlying value might be contribution, mastery, or financial security. Goals get achieved and are done; values are lived and ongoing. Getting clear on values makes it easier to set goals and recover from setbacks.

## Key Questions
- "What would you do if that fear / thought wasn't there?"
- "Has trying to get rid of this feeling worked? What has it cost you?"
- "What does your mind say about this? And what do you — the one watching your mind — actually think?"
- "What matters most to you in this area of your life?"
- "If you imagine a valued version of yourself acting here, what would they do?"
- "What would you need to be willing to feel in order to do what matters?"
- "What story does your mind keep telling you? Does following that story help you live the life you want?"
- "Given that discomfort may be part of this, what is one step you can take anyway?"

## Pitfalls
- **Confusing acceptance with giving up**: Acceptance is making room for feelings; it is not agreeing with a bad situation or stopping efforts to change it.
- **Using defusion to dismiss**: "Just notice your thoughts" can sound dismissive if delivered without empathy. Validate the difficulty before offering the perspective shift.
- **Values as rules**: "I value hard work so I should work harder." Values are directions, not whips. Self-compassion is also a valid value.
- **Skipping to committed action**: Action without acceptance and defusion often hits the same avoidance wall. Spend time on the inner work before assigning behavioural tasks.
- **Clinical boundary**: Full ACT addresses psychopathology (OCD, PTSD, depression). In coaching, focus on flexibility and values in non-clinical populations. Refer when clinical presentations emerge.
- **Hexaflex as a checklist**: These six processes interact and reinforce each other — they are not sequential stages. Follow the thread that is alive in the conversation.
