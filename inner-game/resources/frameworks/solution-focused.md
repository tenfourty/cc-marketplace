## Overview
Solution-focused coaching (SFC) is built on a deceptively simple premise: talking about problems develops problems; talking about solutions develops solutions. Developed by Steve de Shazer and Insoo Kim Berg in brief therapy, it was adapted for coaching by Anthony Grant and others. SFC does not ignore problems — it uses them as a launching pad to explore what the person wants instead, and what is already working that can be amplified.

## When to Use
- User is caught in problem-analysis loops ("Why does this keep happening to me?")
- User has tried problem-focused approaches without success
- User has low confidence or feels helpless — exceptions and strengths rebuild efficacy
- Time-limited sessions where progress needs to happen quickly
- User can articulate what they don't want but not what they do want (miracle question unlocks this)
- User is in a stuck pattern and needs to find the thread that is already working

## When NOT to Use
- Problem truly needs to be understood before solutions are possible (e.g., a technical root cause analysis, a genuine diagnostic puzzle)
- User has unprocessed emotion that needs airtime before forward focus — don't skip emotional acknowledgement
- The "miracle" framing feels absurd or alienating to the user — adapt the language
- Persistent underlying issues (grief, trauma, clinical depression) — refer to qualified support

## The Framework

### Core Assumptions
1. Every person already has resources, strengths, and exceptions to the problem.
2. Small changes can ripple into large shifts — focus on the next small step, not the whole solution.
3. The goal is co-constructed: the coach helps the person clarify what they want, not what the coach thinks they need.
4. "If it ain't broke, don't fix it. If it works, do more of it. If it doesn't work, do something different."

### The Miracle Question
The centrepiece of SFC. It bypasses the problem entirely and invites the person to describe the solution in sensory, concrete detail.

**Classic form:** "Suppose tonight, while you are asleep, a miracle happens and the problem that brought you here is solved. But because you were asleep, you don't know the miracle has happened. When you wake up tomorrow morning, what would be the first thing you notice that tells you the miracle has happened?"

Follow-up probes:
- "What would you be doing differently?"
- "Who else would notice? What would they see?"
- "What is the smallest sign that things are different?"

The miracle question generates a vivid, personal picture of the goal — often more useful than abstract goal-setting.

### Scaling Questions
A versatile tool for measuring progress, confidence, motivation, and next steps.

**Progress scale:** "On a scale of 0–10, where 10 is the miracle and 0 is the worst it's ever been, where are you today?"

Follow with:
- "What has got you to [X] — what's working?" (exception-finding)
- "What would [X+1] look like? What would be different?"
- "What's the smallest thing you could do to move from [X] to [X+1]?"

Scales can also measure readiness, confidence, hope, or any dimension the person finds useful.

### Exception-Finding
Problems are never 100% present. There are always times when the problem is less intense, absent, or managed differently. Exceptions reveal existing strengths and partial solutions.

**Exception questions:**
- "Tell me about a time when this problem wasn't happening — or was less of a problem."
- "What was different about that time? What were you doing differently?"
- "How did you make that happen?"

When exceptions are deliberate (the person did something), amplify the strategy. When they seem accidental ("I'm not sure, it just happened"), help the person identify what they may have done.

### Coping Questions
For users who feel overwhelmed or hopeless — "things can't get better." Coping questions surface the resilience that is already operating beneath their awareness.

- "How have you managed to keep going given everything you're dealing with?"
- "What has stopped this from getting even worse?"
- "How have you coped with difficult situations like this before?"

These questions shift the frame from "I'm failing" to "I'm surviving, and here's how."

### Presupposing Change
Language that assumes positive change has happened or will happen:
- "When things are better, what will be different?" (not "if")
- "What will you notice when you've made progress?"
- "What will other people see you doing differently when you're at [X+2]?"

## Key Questions
- "What would you like to be different as a result of our conversation today?"
- "Suppose a miracle happened tonight and this problem was solved — what would you notice first when you woke up?"
- "On a scale of 0–10, where are you now? What's got you to [X]?"
- "Tell me about a time when this was less of a problem. What was different?"
- "What are you already doing that is working, even a little?"
- "What would [X+1] look like? What's the smallest step toward it?"
- "How have you managed to cope with this so far?"
- "Who else would notice the miracle? What would they see?"

## Pitfalls
- **Toxic positivity**: "Let's focus on solutions!" before the person feels heard reads as dismissive. Acknowledge the difficulty, then pivot to what they want instead.
- **Miracle question literalism**: Some users reject the "miracle" frame. Adapt: "Imagine this problem was somehow resolved overnight..." or use a goal-description question instead.
- **Ignoring real obstacles**: SFC is future-focused, not obstacle-denying. After establishing the goal, ask "What might get in the way?" and problem-solve briefly.
- **Staying only at [X]**: Describing current position is not enough. The value of scaling is in the movement: what would [X+1] look like, and what one small thing would get there?
- **Missing exceptions**: "There are no exceptions — this problem is always there." Push gently: "Even one time, even slightly better?" Exceptions are almost always findable.
