## Overview
CBT-informed coaching applies cognitive-behavioural principles — the relationship between thoughts, feelings, and behaviours — to non-clinical coaching goals. The central insight: it is not events themselves but our interpretation of events that drives how we feel and act. By examining and challenging unhelpful interpretations, people can change both their emotional responses and their behaviour. This is coaching, not therapy — the focus is on performance, development, and goal achievement, not psychological disorder.

## When to Use
- User expresses strong self-limiting beliefs: "I'm not good enough for this," "I always fail at X"
- User is stuck in a clear pattern of avoidance driven by anxiety or fear
- User identifies recurring unhelpful thoughts or inner critic narratives
- Procrastination or perfectionism blocking progress
- User needs practical tools to manage unhelpful thinking between sessions
- User is catastrophising or mind-reading about a situation

## When NOT to Use
- **Clinical presentations**: depression, anxiety disorders, OCD, trauma, eating disorders — refer to a licensed therapist who provides actual CBT
- User is not interested in examining their thinking — don't force cognitive work on someone who wants to focus on action
- The emotional response is entirely proportionate (grief after loss is not a distortion)
- The belief is accurate — not every negative thought is a distortion; some situations genuinely are difficult

**Hard boundary**: CBT-informed coaching works with subclinical thinking habits and performance-related beliefs. If the user describes clinical symptoms, always refer to a qualified mental health professional.

## The Framework

### The Cognitive Model
Events → Thoughts (interpretations) → Feelings → Behaviours → Consequences

The key leverage point is the "Thoughts" link. Same event, different interpretation, different outcome. Coaching works at the interpretation level — helping the person examine whether their current interpretation is accurate, helpful, and serves their goals.

### Common Cognitive Distortions
Recognising these in what the user says is the first step:

- **All-or-nothing thinking**: "If I'm not perfect, I'm a failure"
- **Catastrophising**: "This will be a disaster" / "I can't stand it"
- **Mind reading**: "They think I'm incompetent"
- **Fortune telling**: "I know I'll mess this up"
- **Overgeneralisation**: "I always do this" / "I never get it right"
- **Personalisation**: "It's my fault" (when responsibility is shared or external)
- **Should statements**: "I should be better at this by now" (rigid rules that generate guilt/shame)
- **Emotional reasoning**: "I feel like a fraud, therefore I am one"
- **Discounting the positive**: "That went well but it doesn't count / it was luck"
- **Labelling**: "I'm lazy / stupid / weak" (global self-rating from a behaviour)

### Thought Records (simplified for coaching)
A structured way to examine a specific unhelpful thought:
1. **Situation**: What happened? (objective facts only)
2. **Automatic thought**: What went through your mind? What does this mean about you/others/the future?
3. **Emotion**: What are you feeling? How intense (0–100%)?
4. **Evidence for**: What supports this thought?
5. **Evidence against**: What contradicts it? What would a reasonable person say?
6. **Balanced thought**: A more accurate and helpful interpretation
7. **Outcome**: How do you feel now (0–100%)? What will you do?

In coaching, this is usually done verbally through Socratic questioning — not on paper.

### Socratic Questioning for Beliefs
The primary coaching technique — guided discovery, not lecturing. Help the person reason their way to a more accurate belief rather than being told it's wrong.

Core Socratic moves:
- Clarifying ("What exactly do you mean by that?")
- Evidential ("What evidence supports that? What doesn't?")
- Perspective-shifting ("How would someone who cares about you interpret this?")
- Implication-testing ("If that were true, what would follow? Is that as bad as it sounds?")
- Alternative ("What's another way to look at this?")
- Self-distancing ("What would you say to a friend who thought this about themselves?")

### Behavioural Experiments
Testing beliefs through action rather than analysis. Especially useful when insight alone is not moving the person.

Structure:
1. Identify the belief to test: "If I ask for feedback, people will think less of me"
2. Design a small experiment: ask one trusted colleague for feedback this week
3. Predict the outcome (based on the belief)
4. Run the experiment
5. Review: what actually happened vs the prediction? What does this mean for the belief?

### Perfectionism and Procrastination
These are often linked — perfectionism raises the standard so high that starting feels too risky, leading to avoidance. Key reframes:
- The cost of perfectionism is procrastination, missed opportunities, and exhaustion
- "Good enough" is not settling — it is calibration to what the goal actually requires
- Procrastination is usually avoidance of a feeling (anxiety, shame, boredom), not lack of motivation

## Key Questions
- "What went through your mind when that happened?"
- "What does that mean about you / about this situation?"
- "How true does that feel — 0 to 100%?"
- "What evidence do you have for that thought? What evidence is there against it?"
- "What would you say to a friend who told you they were thinking this?"
- "What's the most realistic outcome here — not the worst, not the best?"
- "Are you treating this feeling as a fact? What would the facts say?"
- "If your prediction were true, how bad would it actually be — and could you cope?"
- "What is this belief costing you? Is it serving your goals?"
- "What small experiment could you run this week to test that assumption?"

## Pitfalls
- **Disputing emotions**: Feelings are not distortions. Only the *interpretation* is up for examination. "You shouldn't feel anxious" is not CBT — it is dismissiveness.
- **Crossing into therapy**: If examining a belief surfaces trauma, grief, or clinical symptoms, stop the thought record and refer. Know the boundary.
- **Telling instead of asking**: "That's catastrophising" is a label, not coaching. Use Socratic questions to guide discovery — the person must reach the reframe themselves.
- **One session, one big belief**: Deep limiting beliefs shift gradually. Don't promise that one conversation changes a core belief held for years.
- **Ignoring behavioural change**: Cognitive insight without behavioural follow-through rarely sticks. Always end with: what will you do differently?
- **Validating accurate negative thoughts**: If the belief is true, don't challenge it. Acknowledge the reality and shift to problem-solving or acceptance.
