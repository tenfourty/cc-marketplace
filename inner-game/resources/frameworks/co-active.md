## Overview
Co-Active Coaching is a comprehensive model developed at CTI (now Co-Active Training Institute) by Laura Whitworth, Henry Kimsey-House, and Phil Sandahl. "Co-Active" captures its dual nature: the coach and client are simultaneously active — co-creators, not expert and recipient. The model rests on four cornerstones and three core principles (fulfillment, balance, process). It is explicitly whole-person coaching — not just addressing presenting goals but the full life of the person in front of you.

## When to Use
- Significant life or career transitions where the person needs to reconnect to what matters
- User's stated goal is entangled with identity, values, or meaning — not just strategy
- User is "successful" by external measures but unfulfilled — fulfillment coaching
- User feels out of balance across life domains — balance coaching
- Emotional undercurrents are present and need to be worked with, not bypassed
- The coach senses something important beneath the surface — "dancing in the moment" is called for
- User needs to be challenged toward their own transformation, not just helped to execute

## When NOT to Use
- User wants focused, structured goal-setting — use GROW (co-active can feel expansive and slow for tactical users)
- User is in crisis and needs stabilisation first — hold the space, don't introduce the model
- User is looking for expertise, mentoring, or advice — co-active is non-directive by design
- Limited time / single-session context where depth work is not possible

## The Framework

### The Four Cornerstones

**1. People are naturally creative, resourceful, and whole**
The foundational assumption that changes everything. The client is not broken. They have what they need. The coach's job is not to fix, advise, or provide answers — it is to evoke the client's own wisdom. This cornerstone demands that the coach constantly trust the client more than the client trusts themselves.

Coaching implication: Never assume you know better. Ask, don't tell. Maintain curiosity even when an "obvious answer" appears.

**2. Focus on the whole person**
Co-active coaching is not problem-focused or goal-focused — it is person-focused. The presenting topic (a project, a decision, a conflict) is the entry point, not the destination. What is happening in the person's life, relationships, body, energy, and sense of purpose is all relevant data.

Coaching implication: Notice what is not being said. Ask about dimensions the client has not mentioned. "What's happening in the rest of your life while this is going on?"

**3. Dance in the moment**
Sessions follow the energy of what is alive, not a pre-set agenda. The coach tracks what is emerging — a shift in emotion, an unexpected statement, a tangent that is not really a tangent — and goes there. The metaphor of dance: responsive, attuned, present. No fixed choreography.

Coaching implication: Let go of the plan. The most important thing in this conversation is usually not on the agenda. Follow the energy. "I noticed something shift when you said that — what's happening?"

**4. Evoke transformation**
Co-active coaching aims for more than incremental improvement. It seeks shifts in perspective, identity, and possibility. Transformation means the person sees themselves or their situation differently — not just knows more or does more. The coach holds this horizon even when the client is focused on the immediate.

Coaching implication: Ask big questions. Challenge limiting narratives. Don't settle for the first or safest answer. "Is this the biggest vision you can hold for yourself right now?"

### The Designed Alliance
At the start of a coaching relationship, coach and client explicitly design how they will work together. This is not a contract — it is a living agreement, renegotiated as needed. It includes:
- What the client needs from the coach (challenge, support, accountability, structure)
- What kind of feedback is useful and how directly to give it
- What success looks like for them
- Permissions: "You have permission to interrupt me when I'm going in circles"

The Designed Alliance makes the relationship a conscious tool rather than an implicit, unexamined one.

### Three Core Principles

**Fulfillment**
Coaching into what the person finds meaningful, purposeful, and alive. Fulfillment is not happiness — it is the experience of living in alignment with one's deepest values and commitments. Fulfillment coaching asks: "What would make this life feel fully lived?"

Key moves:
- Values clarification: "What matters most to you at this point in your life?"
- Gap analysis: "On a scale of 0–10, how fulfilled do you feel in this area right now? What's the gap?"
- Sabotage patterns: "What do you do that gets in the way of your own fulfillment?"

**Balance**
Not work-life balance in the thin sense, but the capacity to see the full picture of one's life and make conscious choices. Balance coaching challenges the client to see what they are over-investing in and what they are neglecting — and to choose deliberately.

Key moves:
- Wheel of Life (adapted): rate satisfaction across life domains (work, relationships, health, creativity, community, etc.)
- "If you shifted one thing in this picture, what would have the most impact?"
- Challenge the trade-offs being made unconsciously: "You say this matters to you — where is it in your calendar?"

**Process**
Emotions, energy, and what is happening *right now* in the conversation are data, not noise. Process coaching works with what is present — an emotion that surfaces, a moment of resistance, an unexpected laugh or silence — rather than bypassing it to get back to content.

Key moves:
- Acknowledge what is present: "I notice something shifted — what's happening?"
- Name what you observe: "There's a lot of energy in your voice when you talk about this"
- Work with the emotion directly: "What's underneath that frustration?"

### Level 3 Listening
CTI distinguishes three listening levels:
- **Level 1**: Listening to your own thoughts, agenda, and reactions (internal focus)
- **Level 2**: Focused on the client's words and meaning (content)
- **Level 3**: Global listening — attending to everything: tone, energy, what's unsaid, what's in the room

Co-active coaching requires Level 3. The coach listens with full attention to the person as a whole, including what is present in the space between words.

## Key Questions
- "What do you want?" (The most powerful co-active question — asked repeatedly as depth increases)
- "What would make this feel fully lived?"
- "What are you tolerating that you shouldn't be?"
- "What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?"
- "What is your life asking of you right now?"
- "If you weren't afraid, what would you choose?"
- "What are you not saying that needs to be said?"
- "What is the invitation in this situation?"
- "Who do you need to be — not what do you need to do — to get through this?"
- "What would it look like to stop settling?"
- "I noticed something just now when you said that — what's happening inside you?"
- "What's the biggest vision you have for yourself here? And then what's the even bigger one?"

## Pitfalls
- **Being too spacious**: Co-active can become unfocused without structure. If the client is spinning, introduce a simple focus: "Of everything we've touched on, what's most important right now?"
- **Imposing depth**: Not every client wants or needs transformation. Some want task completion. Read the person, not the model.
- **Soft challenge**: "Evoking transformation" means real challenge. The co-active coach calls the client forward, challenges their assumptions, and does not settle for the safe answer. Warmth without challenge is support, not coaching.
- **Skipping the Designed Alliance**: Without it, the coach guesses at what the client needs. A 10-minute conversation about how to work together saves many sessions of misalignment.
- **Forgetting the whole person**: It is easy to get pulled into the presenting problem. Periodically zoom out: "We've been focused on X — what's happening in the rest of your life?"
- **Level 1 listening throughout**: If the coach is planning the next question while the client speaks, they are at Level 1. The next question should emerge from deep listening, not preparation.
