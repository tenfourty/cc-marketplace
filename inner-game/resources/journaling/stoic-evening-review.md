## Overview
The Stoic Evening Review is a nightly written examination of conscience drawn from ancient Stoic practice. Seneca describes his own version in "On Anger": before sleep, he reviewed his entire day through three questions — what did he do well, what could he improve, and what did he leave undone. The practice is non-judgmental by design: the Stoic goal is honest self-examination without self-flagellation. Marcus Aurelius practiced a similar review throughout the "Meditations." The underlying philosophy is that each day is a complete unit of life, worthy of examination on its own terms.

## When to Use
- As a nightly anchor practice (10-15 minutes before sleep)
- When you want to close the loop on a day rather than carrying it, unexamined, into tomorrow
- Periods of high pressure or conflict where you want to track your conduct relative to your values
- When building a character-based identity (how am I actually behaving, not just how do I intend to behave)
- As complement to a morning intention practice — the evening review closes what the morning opened
- During any major challenge where maintaining virtue and equanimity matters

## When NOT to Use
- As a tool for self-punishment or rumination — if you find yourself spiraling into guilt, redirect the practice toward what you will do tomorrow rather than what you failed to do today
- Immediately after a major acute trauma — basic grounding first
- As a replacement for processing conflict with others (journaling about a difficult interaction is useful; avoiding the actual conversation is not)

## The Practice

**Timing:** Within 30-60 minutes of sleep. Seneca writes of doing this in bed, by lamplight, after his wife had fallen asleep. A quiet, low-stimulus environment is ideal.

**Medium:** A physical notebook is traditional and keeps you off screens before sleep. Even a few lines is sufficient. Three to ten sentences per question is a reasonable target.

**The three questions — write a response to each:**

**1. What did I do well today?**
Not what went well externally — what did *you* do well, by your own standard? Where did you act with integrity, courage, patience, or discipline? Where were you the person you want to be? This question trains the habit of noticing your own positive conduct, not just your failures. Seneca emphasizes this: the review is not only a reckoning but an encouragement.

**2. What could I have done better?**
Where did you fall short of your own standard? Not where life failed you — where did *you* fail to act according to your values? Be honest and specific. Name the moment, the action, the choice. But do not dwell: identify it, understand why it happened, and let it go. The Stoics distinguish between useful regret (which informs tomorrow) and useless self-punishment (which serves no one).

**3. What did I leave undone?**
What did you intend to do and did not? What conversation did you defer? What did you know you should address but avoided? This is not a to-do list — it is an honesty check. If the same item appears here repeatedly, that is important information.

**Optional: The Stoic coda**
After the three questions, write one sentence beginning with "Tomorrow I will..." — a single, specific intention drawn from what the review revealed. Not a grand resolution: one small concrete action.

**On amor fati:** The Stoic practice includes accepting what cannot be changed. After honest review, the appropriate posture is: I see this clearly, I take responsibility for what was mine, I release what was not. The review ends with sleep, not with continued mulling.

**Time required:** 10-20 minutes. Can be compressed to 5 minutes (one sentence per question) on difficult nights.

## Key Prompts

For Question 1 (What did I do well?):
- "Where today did I act according to my values even when it was inconvenient?"
- "What small act of courage, patience, or honesty did I manage today?"
- "Who did I show up for today, and how?"
- "What did I handle better than I would have a year ago?"

For Question 2 (What could I improve?):
- "Where did I react when I should have paused?"
- "Was I honest today — with others, and with myself?"
- "Where did I let fear or comfort override what I knew was right?"
- "How did I treat the people around me when I was under pressure?"
- "What did I say that I wish I had said differently?"

For Question 3 (What did I leave undone?):
- "What did I know I should address today that I deferred?"
- "Whose message did I not return? Why?"
- "What conversation am I avoiding?"
- "Where did I substitute busyness for the thing that actually mattered?"

For integration:
- "What is the one thing I want to remember about today?"
- "If today were my last day, would I feel I had lived it well enough? If not, what one thing would change?"

## Pitfalls

**Turning it into rumination.** The review is a scan, not a deep-dive into every failure. Name, understand briefly, and release. If you find yourself returning to the same event repeatedly, that signals something that needs direct action — a conversation, a repair, a decision — not more journaling.

**Only reviewing failures.** Many practitioners focus entirely on Question 2 and skip Question 1. This produces a negative attention bias. Seneca was explicit: note what you did well. The practice is a full accounting, not a penance.

**Making it abstract.** "I could have been kinder" is less useful than "I was short with the person at the counter when I was running late, and that wasn't necessary." Specificity is what makes the review actionable.

**Doing it on a screen.** Late-screen stimulation suppresses sleep onset. The Stoics wrote by hand, and for practical reasons the tradition holds. Use paper.

**Skipping when the day was bad.** The impulse to avoid reviewing a difficult day is exactly backwards. The days when you fell furthest short of your values are the days the practice matters most. Brief and honest is better than avoided.

**Treating consistency as optional.** One review a week is interesting. Nightly review for thirty days changes how you move through days — because you know you will account for them. The accountability loop is the mechanism.
