## Overview
The Five Minute Journal is a structured two-session daily practice: a brief morning entry that primes intention and gratitude, and an evening entry that reviews the day and extracts one lesson. The format is fixed — always the same five prompts in the same order — which eliminates the blank-page problem and keeps the total time under five minutes. Developed by UJ Ramdas and Alex Ikonn, it is grounded in positive psychology research on gratitude, intentionality, and reflective practice.

## When to Use
- Beginners who find open-ended journaling intimidating or inconsistent
- High-output periods when you have no time for longer practices
- As a daily anchor when other practices (Morning Pages, Stoic review) are suspended due to travel or schedule disruption
- When you want to build a consistent daily journaling habit through low-friction structure
- When mood and outlook are persistently negative and you want a systematic intervention
- Pairs well with habit stacking: attach to coffee preparation (morning) and tooth-brushing (evening)

## When NOT to Use
- As the only journaling practice when processing complex emotions or major decisions (the format is too constrained for depth)
- When you need creative unblocking (use Morning Pages instead)
- When the prompts start feeling mechanical — that is a signal to supplement with open-ended writing or a prompt library
- As a substitute for therapy or professional support during crisis

## The Practice

**Morning entry (2-3 minutes)**
Complete immediately upon waking or during the first 15 minutes of your day. Before checking any device.

**Evening entry (1-2 minutes)**
Complete within 30 minutes of going to sleep. Review while the day is still fresh.

**Total time per day:** Under 5 minutes across both sessions.

**The exact prompts — do not vary them:**

Morning (write before the day begins):
1. I am grateful for... (3 specific things)
2. What would make today great? (3 specific intentions)
3. Daily affirmation. I am... (1 statement about who you are or are becoming)

Evening (write before sleep):
4. 3 amazing things that happened today... (3 highlights — can be small)
5. How could I have made today even better? (1 honest reflection)

**Format:** Use a dedicated physical notebook or the official journal product. Keep entries brief — one line per item is sufficient. Depth is not the goal here; consistency and specificity are.

**On specificity (critical):** "My family" as a gratitude entry has less psychological impact than "The conversation with my sister where she made me laugh for ten minutes." Specificity creates genuine emotional contact with the experience. Generic entries become rote quickly.

**On the affirmation:** Write in present tense as if already true. "I am someone who keeps their word" rather than "I want to be someone who keeps their word." Aspirational identity framing, grounded in evidence from your own life.

## Key Prompts

The format is fixed, but these variations help when the standard prompts feel stale:

For gratitude:
- "I'm grateful for the moment today when..."
- "Something small that I usually overlook but actually matters is..."
- "One person I'm grateful for right now, and specifically why..."

For daily intentions:
- "One thing I could do today that my future self would thank me for is..."
- "What's the one outcome that would make everything else feel secondary?"

For evening highlights:
- "A moment today that I want to remember is..."
- "Something that went better than I expected was..."

For the lesson:
- "If I could replay one moment from today differently, it would be..."
- "What did today teach me about what I actually value?"

## Pitfalls

**Generic gratitude entries.** "Health, family, food" every morning is the enemy of the practice. These entries stop producing any psychological effect within days. Force specificity: which meal, which interaction, which small pleasure.

**Skipping the evening entry.** Many people do the morning and drop the evening. The evening review is where the learning loop closes. Without it, you are practicing optimism without reflection.

**Using it as a to-do list.** "What would make today great" is not a task list — it is about intention and presence, not productivity metrics. Keep the focus on experience, not output.

**Treating the affirmation as magic.** Affirmations work through identity reinforcement, not wishful thinking. Ground them in actual evidence: "I am someone who shows up for hard things" works if you can point to examples. Purely aspirational statements without behavioral grounding tend to backfire.

**Mechanical completion.** When filling in boxes replaces genuine engagement, the practice has become a habit of the hand rather than the mind. Pause when this happens. Take one extra minute. Read your last week's entries before writing today's.

**Never reviewing past entries.** Unlike Morning Pages, this journal is worth re-reading. Monthly review of past entries reveals patterns: recurring gratitudes, unmet intentions, lessons you keep needing to relearn.
