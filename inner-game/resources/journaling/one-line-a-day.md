## Overview
One Line a Day is the minimalist floor of journaling practice: a single sentence written each day, every day. The constraint is the point. By reducing the entry requirement to one sentence, the practice removes the primary reason journaling lapses — the feeling that if you can't write a real entry, you shouldn't write at all. Applied to journaling, James Clear's principle from "Atomic Habits" is exact: never miss twice, and make the minimum so small it can't be skipped. Over months and years, a one-line-a-day record becomes a surprisingly powerful artifact — a compressed map of your life that allows you to see patterns invisible at daily resolution.

## When to Use
- Building or rebuilding a journaling habit after a lapse (use as the foundation, not the ceiling)
- During high-demand periods (travel, crunch, new baby, illness) when longer practices are genuinely impossible
- As a daily anchor that you can extend when you have more time, or leave as one line when you don't
- To create a long-term record without the pressure of producing "good" entries
- When perfectionism is the specific barrier — the format pre-empts perfectionism by design
- As a complement to longer practices: even on Morning Pages days, a one-line summary closes the day

## When NOT to Use
- As a complete journaling practice for someone who wants depth and insight — it is a minimum, not a method
- When you are using journaling to process complex emotions or decisions (use Morning Pages, Fear Setting, or open-ended prompts instead)
- When you are tempted to make the one line "good" — if you are editing the sentence, the method has become self-defeating

## The Practice

**The rule:** Write one sentence, every day, no exceptions.

One sentence means: a complete thought. Not a fragment, not a to-do, not a word. One sentence that captures something true about the day.

**Timing:** Consistent time is more important than which time. Many practitioners write their line as the last thing before sleep. Others write it at the same time each morning about the previous day. Choose a time that already has an anchor (toothbrushing, making coffee, closing the laptop) and attach the one-line entry to it.

**Format options:**

Option A: Pure capture — What happened or what I noticed
> "First day working from the new space — quieter than expected, finished more than usual."

Option B: One feeling or state — What I felt most strongly today
> "Unexpectedly moved by a conversation I didn't see coming."

Option C: One insight or question — What I'm carrying with me
> "I keep agreeing to things I don't want to do because the 'no' feels complicated in the moment."

Option D: One thing I'm grateful for — The gratitude minimum
> "The particular quality of light at 6pm today through the kitchen window — genuinely stopped me."

Option E: Your own recurring prompt — Invent a frame you want to track
> "Best moment: [X]. Worst moment: [X]." or "Energy today: [X]."

**The one-line-plus extension:** The practice works as a floor, not a ceiling. On days when you have more to say, write more — but never write less than the one line. This asymmetry is deliberate. Days with nothing to say produce one line. Days with something to say produce a paragraph. The chain never breaks.

**Physical format:** The Chronicle Books "One Line a Day" journal is designed for this practice — five years, one page per calendar date, with five boxes stacked so the same date across years appears together. This format enables time-travel comparisons: reading March 3rd entries from five consecutive years on a single page. This longitudinal view is the distinctive value of the practice. A plain notebook with dates works equally well.

**The chain:** Mark each completed day on a calendar or in a habit tracker. Do not break the chain. When you miss (which will happen), resume immediately. "Never miss twice" — Clear's rule — is the operative principle.

**Time required:** 60 seconds on a minimal day. No upper limit.

## Key Prompts

If you are staring at a blank page:
- "The thing I will most remember about today is..."
- "One true thing about today:"
- "Right now I feel..."
- "Today I learned / noticed / decided..."
- "One sentence I want my future self to know about today:"

For recurring frames (pick one and use it consistently):
- "Best and hardest of today: [X] / [X]"
- "Today's energy: [1-5]. Today's one moment: [X]"
- "What I made happen: [X]"
- "One thing I did that I'm proud of / one thing I'd do differently:"

For the long-term record:
- "In one sentence, what kind of day was this?"
- "One year from today, what will matter about what happened today?"
- "If I told someone about today in one sentence at a dinner party, I'd say..."

## Pitfalls

**Treating it as optional.** The moment the one-line entry becomes optional on hard days, the practice collapses. The rule is unconditional: one line, every day, no exceptions. On the worst days, "Today was hard" is a complete and valid entry.

**Making it a performance.** The one-line entry is not for posterity. It is not for anyone else to read. When you start crafting the sentence for a hypothetical reader, the practice has drifted. Write for yourself, honestly, in plain language.

**Stopping at one line when you have more to say.** The method is a minimum, not a maximum. Many of the most useful journal entries in a one-line practice start as the required sentence and unfold into paragraphs. Do not close the notebook if there is more to write.

**Losing the longitudinal view.** The compounding value of this practice is in reviewing past entries. If you use a dated notebook or the Chronicle format, build a regular review into your practice: monthly, quarterly, or on each anniversary. The patterns visible across months and years are invisible at the daily level.

**Substituting it for deeper work during periods that actually need it.** One line a day is the minimum viable practice. It is not a substitute for processing a major loss, navigating a significant decision, or working through a complex emotion. Know when the minimum is insufficient and reach for a fuller practice.

**Perfectionism about the sentence.** The sentence does not need to be well-written. It does not need to be insightful. It needs to be honest and written today. "Nothing much today, tired" is a legitimate entry. The chronicle of ordinary days is exactly as valuable as the chronicle of exceptional ones — more so, because ordinary days are the majority of a life.
