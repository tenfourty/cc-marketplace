## Overview
Interstitial journaling is a practice of writing a timestamped entry every time you switch between tasks or contexts — at the gaps between things, rather than only at the beginning and end of the day. Developed and popularized by Tony Stubblebine (CEO of Medium, founder of Coach.me), the method addresses a key limitation of morning and evening journaling: by the time you sit down to reflect, most of the texture of your day has already been lost. Writing in the interstitial moments — between meetings, after completing a task, before switching contexts — captures decisions, mental states, and energy levels while they are still live.

## When to Use
- Knowledge workers with fragmented days (many meetings, frequent context switching)
- When you suspect your energy, focus, or mood varies significantly throughout the day but you can't see the pattern
- During a period of high-volume decision-making where you want an audit trail of your reasoning
- When you want to combine time tracking with reflection (interstitial entries function as both)
- When morning/evening journaling alone is insufficient to capture the complexity of your days
- As a real-time debrief practice for each meeting or deep work block

## When NOT to Use
- During creative deep work sessions where interruptions undermine flow — the practice is for transitions, not the middle of focused work
- On days with no natural task transitions (rare, but some work has this character)
- As a replacement for end-of-day review — interstitial capture needs a brief synthesis at day's end to be fully useful
- When you are in an environment where stopping to write every hour would be socially or professionally disruptive

## The Practice

**The core rule:** Write before you switch. Every time you move from one task or context to another — meeting ends, focus session completes, you break for lunch, you shift from email to a project — open your journal and write a timestamped entry before doing anything else.

**Format of each entry:**
```
[TIME] — [What I just finished / where I'm transitioning from]
[1-3 sentences about the task, decision, or mental state]
[What's next / what I'm about to do]
```

Example entries:
```
09:17 — Post-standup
Team seemed tense about the deadline. Noticed I defaulted to optimism to smooth it over rather than naming the tension. Need to actually check in with Priya before the afternoon.
Next: 90-min deep work block on the architecture doc.

11:52 — Architecture doc → lunch
Got the core structure down. Slower than I wanted. Three decision points I kept circling — wrote them at the bottom of the doc but didn't resolve. That circularity cost me 40 minutes.
Next: Lunch, then clear email before the 2pm call.

14:08 — Post-client call
They pushed back harder than expected on the timeline. I agreed to something I'm not confident about. Need to flag this to the team before it becomes a commitment we can't keep.
Next: Write the internal summary while this is fresh.
```

**What to capture in each entry:**
- The emotional or mental texture of what just happened
- Decisions made and the reasoning behind them (even briefly)
- Things you noticed about yourself — reactions, avoidances, surprises
- Energy level (optional: use a 1-5 scale for pattern analysis)
- Loose ends or intentions before the next context

**Timestamping:** Essential. The timestamps allow you to reconstruct the day's actual shape — not the planned shape, the actual one. They also enable time tracking if that is useful to you.

**Tools:** A physical notebook works well. Digital options that work: plain text file with timestamps, Obsidian daily note, any text editor. Avoid tools with friction (don't open a new app, start a new document). The journal lives open, ready to receive entries.

**Day close:** At end of day, take 5 minutes to read through all entries and write 3-5 sentences of synthesis. What was the actual shape of the day? What pattern do you see? What carries over?

**Time required per entry:** 2-5 minutes. Total daily time: 15-40 minutes spread across the day.

## Key Prompts

For task transitions:
- "What just happened, and what am I actually thinking about it?"
- "What decisions did I make in the last block, and why?"
- "What am I carrying into the next thing that I should put down?"
- "What did I avoid doing in that last block?"

For energy tracking:
- "My energy right now is [1-5]. What is driving that?"
- "What drained me in the last hour? What energized me?"
- "Am I choosing the next task based on what matters, or based on how I feel?"

For meeting transitions (immediately post-meeting):
- "What was the real subtext of that conversation?"
- "What did I agree to, exactly? Do I stand behind it?"
- "What do I need to do in the next 24 hours to follow through?"
- "What would have made that meeting more useful?"

For end-of-day synthesis:
- "Looking at all my entries today, what was I actually doing vs. what I planned to do?"
- "Where did my attention go that I didn't intend?"
- "What pattern do I see about when I was most effective?"
- "What one thing from today am I going to handle differently tomorrow?"

## Pitfalls

**Writing only events, not reactions.** "Had the standup, then worked on the report, then had lunch" is a log, not a journal. The value is in capturing your internal experience — the frustration, the insight, the avoidance, the decision and its reasoning. Force yourself into the first person and the present tense.

**Letting the timestamps disappear.** Entries without timestamps are harder to use for pattern analysis and harder to reconstruct into a coherent day. Timestamp every entry, even if it's just approximate.

**Writing too much.** Interstitial entries should be brief — 3-7 sentences at most. This is not Morning Pages. If you are spending 10+ minutes on an entry, you are either writing too much or the topic needs its own dedicated session.

**Skipping the day-close synthesis.** Individual entries are raw material. The synthesis is where the learning lives. Without it, you have a detailed log but no insight.

**Writing between tasks but not reviewing across days.** The real power of interstitial journaling is longitudinal: comparing Tuesday's entry pattern with last Tuesday's, noticing that you always crash after certain meeting types, seeing that your best deep work consistently happens in the same window. Weekly review of your daily entries dramatically multiplies the return on the daily practice.

**Treating it as a to-do list.** The "what's next" field at the end of each entry is a context note, not a task list. Keep it to one line. If you are writing long task lists in your journal, use your task manager.
