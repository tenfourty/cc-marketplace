## Overview

The Document is not fixed. As people grow, navigate major transitions, and deepen their self-understanding, some declarations may need revision — either because they have been fully inhabited and need to be deepened, or because life circumstances have shifted what they stand for, or because a declaration was never fully honest and that has become visible over time. Revision is not failure. It is evidence that The Document is doing its job. The goal of revision is to keep The Document accurate: a statement of who you genuinely are, not a monument to who you used to be trying to become.

## When to Use

- User reports that specific declarations consistently feel stale, hollow, or no longer fitting
- User has experienced a major life transition (new role, relationship change, significant loss or gain, relocation, identity shift)
- User's evening alignment scores have been persistently low (≤5) on specific declarations for 2+ weeks without a clear situational explanation
- User has grown significantly beyond specific declarations — they have fully inhabited them and need to deepen them
- Scheduled anniversary review (every 6-12 months)
- User reports The Document as a whole no longer feeling like them

## When NOT to Use

- The Document is relatively new (less than 3 months) and the user is simply in the adjustment period — gently hold to the practice first
- User wants to revise because declarations feel hard — difficulty is not a revision trigger; it is a sign the declaration is working
- User is in the middle of acute stress and wants to revise declarations that feel impossible right now — wait for the situation to stabilize
- User wants to add declarations because they just read something inspiring — inspiration is not a revision trigger

## The Core Principle: Revision vs Abandonment

The most important distinction in this process is between revising The Document and abandoning it.

**Revision** means: this declaration no longer accurately captures who I am or what I stand for. I am going to update the language, deepen it, or in rare cases replace it with something more accurate.

**Abandonment** means: this declaration is hard to live and I want to take it out so I don't have to feel bad about not living it. This is not revision. It is capitulation.

The test: "Is this declaration being removed because it is no longer true, or because it is inconvenient?" If inconvenient — keep it, but examine why it is being resisted. If genuinely no longer accurate — revise.

## Revision Triggers in Detail

### Trigger 1: Major Life Transition
A major transition changes the context in which you live your declarations. A new role, a significant relationship change, a loss, a move to a different country, a health crisis, becoming a parent, leaving a career — these events can shift what matters most and what you are standing for.

After a major transition, do a full Document review. The question for each declaration: "Is this still accurate for who I am in this new context? Or does it need to be updated to reflect where I am now?"

Some declarations survive transitions unchanged — they are fundamental enough to apply regardless of context. Others need to be updated to reflect the new terrain.

### Trigger 2: Persistent Low Alignment
If the evening alignment check consistently returns scores of 5 or below on a specific declaration for 2+ weeks — and there is no clear external explanation (acute stress, unusual circumstances) — this is a signal that something has shifted.

There are three possible diagnoses:
1. **The declaration is aspirational, not actual.** It never was fully honest — it captured where the user hoped to be rather than where they stood. This may have been right at the time of creation. Revise toward truth.
2. **The declaration no longer fits who the user is becoming.** They have shifted and this declaration is from an earlier version of themselves. Update it to capture who they actually are now.
3. **The user is genuinely failing to live a declaration they still believe in.** This is not a revision trigger — this is an integration challenge. Distinguish carefully: do you still believe this is who you are? If yes, don't revise. Work on the practice.

### Trigger 3: Growth Beyond a Declaration
Sometimes a declaration has been fully inhabited — the user lives it so naturally that it no longer represents a meaningful anchor point. This is success, not a problem. But a declaration that feels entirely trivial is no longer doing its job.

Signs of this: "When I read this, I think — of course. That's just who I am. It doesn't feel like anything anymore."

For these declarations, the choice is: deepen or retire. Deepening means finding a more specific, more demanding version of the same commitment. Retiring means acknowledging it is now fully integrated and removing it from The Document — its work here is done.

### Trigger 4: Anniversary Review
Every 6-12 months, conduct a deliberate Document review regardless of whether any triggers have appeared. This is a scheduled check-in with the whole, not a reactive response to a specific problem.

The anniversary review process is the same as the full revision process below.

## The Revision Process

### Step 1: Read The Current Document Aloud
Sit down alone, with no agenda to change anything yet. Read The Document aloud, slowly, exactly as you do every morning. Then sit with it for a moment.

Notice: What still resonates immediately? What feels slightly off? What feels entirely natural? What feels like it belongs to a previous version of you? What feels like it could go deeper?

Do not analyze yet. Just notice.

### Step 2: Mark the Declarations
After the reading, go through each declaration and assign it to one of three categories:

- **Anchor** — still fully resonant, no change needed
- **Evolving** — the intention is right but the language needs updating, or the declaration needs to be deepened
- **Revisit** — something about this one has shifted; needs more examination

### Step 3: Examine the "Revisit" Declarations
For each declaration in the "Revisit" category, run the following examination:

- "What was this declaration saying when I wrote it? What was it pointing at?"
- "Does that same intention still matter to me? If yes — does this language still capture it? If not — what has changed?"
- "Am I flagging this because it no longer fits, or because it is uncomfortable to live? If uncomfortable: why? What is that discomfort pointing at?"
- "If I were writing this declaration today, from scratch, what would I write?"

Write the "today" version and compare to the existing version. Sometimes they are nearly identical — the declaration was fine and needed only minor language polish. Sometimes the "today" version is significantly different — indicating genuine growth or shift.

### Step 4: Examine the "Evolving" Declarations
For each declaration in the "Evolving" category:

- "What is the truer, deeper, or more specific version of this?"
- "Has this declaration been fully inhabited — and if so, what is the next frontier of it?"
- "What word or phrase in the current version feels slightly off? What would be more accurate?"

Work these declarations the same way as new declarations in Phase 4 — read them aloud, apply the "solid ground" test, refine language until they land.

### Step 5: Consider What's Missing
Revision is also an opportunity to ask whether something genuinely new belongs in The Document.

After a major transition or period of growth, there may be a new domain of identity that has emerged and wants to be declared. Apply the same Phase 4 test: "Is this a genuine declaration — does it feel true when I say it? Or is this an aspiration — something I want to move toward?"

Do not add new declarations out of enthusiasm. The "new declaration" test: read it aloud. If it feels like solid ground, add it. If it feels like reaching, it is not ready.

### Step 6: Assemble the Revised Document
Write the full revised Document — incorporating anchors unchanged, updated versions of evolving declarations, and any new declarations. Read it in full aloud.

The complete aloud test applies to the whole: "Does this sound like one person? Does it hold together? Is there anything here that feels performative?"

Refine until the full reading feels coherent and grounded.

### Step 7: Acknowledge What Is Being Released
If any declarations are being removed or significantly changed, acknowledge this consciously. Spend a moment with what the old declaration meant and why it was there. Then release it deliberately.

This is not ceremony for its own sake — it is preventing the passive carrying of an old Document alongside the new one. The old version is complete. The new version begins now.

## Revision vs Starting Over

Starting over — going back through all five phases — is occasionally warranted. It is almost never necessary.

Starting over is appropriate when:
- The user has gone through a transformation so fundamental that the entire values hierarchy has shifted and the Document bears no resemblance to who they are
- The Document was originally created poorly — the phases were rushed and the declarations were never fully honest — and revision cannot fix something that was never right

In all other cases, revision is the right process. The foundation work of Phases 1-3 does not expire. The values hierarchy may need to be revisited but rarely needs to be rebuilt from scratch. The declarations need updating, not replacing.

If the user insists on starting over, ask: "Is the impulse to start over about the Document being genuinely outdated, or about wanting a fresh start because you feel you've been failing this version?" If the latter, revision is still the right process.

## Key Questions

- "For each declaration you've flagged: is this no longer accurate, or is it just uncomfortable? What's the difference in how those feel when you sit with them?"
- "What has changed in your life or your understanding of yourself since you wrote this?"
- "Which declarations have you fully inhabited? What would a deeper version of those declarations look like?"
- "Is there something important about who you are now that has no declaration yet?"
- "When you read the revised Document aloud in full — does it sound like you? Does it feel like the ground you are standing on?"

## Completion Criteria

Revision is complete when:
1. The full revised Document has been read aloud and every declaration passes the "solid ground" test
2. The user can explain why each declaration is in the Document — either "this is fully accurate" or "this is where I am growing"
3. There are no declarations that feel like they belong to a previous version of the user without a conscious choice to keep them
4. The revised Document feels like a living, accurate statement of current identity — not a museum piece and not a wish list

## Pitfalls

**Revising to escape.** Removing a declaration because it is hard to live is not revision. It is lowering the bar. Distinguish: "This declaration is no longer accurate" (revise) from "This declaration is accurate but I keep failing it" (keep it and work on the practice).

**Revising too frequently.** Revision triggered by a bad week, an inspiring book, or an exciting conversation is reactive rather than responsive. The Document should have some stability. Most revisions should be prompted by sustained patterns over weeks, not days.

**Treating revision as starting over.** Going back to Phase 1 every time a declaration needs updating is unnecessary. Revise the specific declaration. The foundation is intact.

**Adding too much during revision.** The appetite during a revision session can be to add many new declarations reflecting current growth areas. Apply the same discipline as Phase 4: does this feel true when I say it aloud? If it is still aspirational, it is not ready.

**Not acknowledging what is being released.** Silently changing declarations without acknowledging what the previous version meant can leave a sense of incompleteness. Acknowledge the previous declaration, honor what it was doing, and consciously close it before moving to the new one.
