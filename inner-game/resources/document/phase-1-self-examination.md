## Overview

Self-examination is the first phase of creating The Document. It is not therapy, not journaling for its own sake, and not an exercise in self-criticism. It is precise inquiry into the identity structures that are already running — the beliefs, patterns, and inner critic voices that currently shape who you are being. You cannot declare a new identity without first seeing clearly what identity is already in place.

## When to Use

- User is ready to begin The Document journey and hasn't done Phase 1 yet
- User is drafting declarations that feel hollow — may need to return to this phase
- User is discovering a persistent belief that keeps surfacing in daily practice

## When NOT to Use

- User has already done deep self-examination recently and is ready to move to Phase 2
- User is in acute emotional distress — stabilize first
- User wants to rush to declarations — explain that this phase is the foundation, not optional overhead

## Estimated Duration

2-3 sessions, with reflection work (journaling) between sessions. Allow 1-3 weeks total. This phase cannot be completed in a single sitting — the work needs time to surface.

## The Core Distinction: Seeing vs Fixing

Phase 1 is about seeing, not fixing. The goal is not to solve the patterns you discover — it is to name them accurately, without flinching and without judgment. Patterns cannot be transcended while they remain invisible. The moment they are seen clearly, they lose some of their automatic power.

Introduce this framing at the start: "We are not here to fix anything. We are here to see what is already running."

## The Inner Critic (Self 1)

In The Document framework, the inner critic is sometimes called "Self 1" — the voice of interference that narrates failure, predicts danger, and enforces old stories. Self 1 is not the enemy. It is a protection mechanism that was once useful and now often isn't.

Self 1 says things like:
- "You always let people down eventually."
- "You're not as capable as people think."
- "If they knew the real you, they'd leave."
- "You don't deserve this."

Naming Self 1's specific language is a significant piece of Phase 1 work. The user needs to be able to hear Self 1's voice and recognize it as a voice — not as truth.

## Core Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Inner Critic Inventory
Ask the user to write down every negative thing their inner critic says about them. Not a curated list — every ugly, repetitive, embarrassing one. Give them permission to be completely honest. If the list feels uncomfortable, that is the right list.

Questions to surface the inner critic:
- "What does the voice in your head say when you make a mistake?"
- "What do you believe about yourself that you would never say to a good friend?"
- "What is the thing about yourself you most hope others never figure out?"
- "If your inner critic wrote your obituary, what would it say?"

Completion signal: The user can read their inner critic inventory aloud without flinching or defending. They are observing it, not identifying with it.

### Exercise 2: Repeating Patterns
Invite the user to look across their life for patterns that keep repeating — not to find fault, but to find information.

Questions:
- "What patterns keep showing up in your life, regardless of the circumstances changing?"
- "What do your last three failed [relationships / jobs / projects] have in common?"
- "Where do you keep arriving at the same outcome despite your best intentions?"
- "Complete this sentence: 'In moments of real pressure, I always ______.'"

Record the patterns without evaluation. The goal is a list, not a story.

### Exercise 3: Inherited vs Chosen Beliefs
Many beliefs about identity were absorbed in childhood and were never consciously chosen. This exercise separates what was handed to you from what you would actually choose.

Questions:
- "What beliefs about yourself did you receive from your family of origin?"
- "What were you told you were, repeatedly, as a child?"
- "Which of those beliefs are you still acting from — even if you would consciously reject them?"
- "If you could choose your beliefs about yourself freely today, which of the ones you were given would you keep?"

This is often where significant material surfaces. Proceed gently.

### Exercise 4: Completion Sentences
These sentence completions surface automatic beliefs quickly. Ask the user to complete them without thinking — first instinct only.

- "I am someone who always ______."
- "I am someone who never ______."
- "When things get hard, I ______."
- "People can count on me to ______."
- "People cannot count on me to ______."
- "I am not the kind of person who ______."
- "The thing I keep promising myself and breaking is ______."

Review the answers together. Where is there energy? Where is there avoidance? The avoidance is usually the important material.

### Exercise 5: The Current Identity Statement
Ask the user to write a 3-5 sentence description of who they currently are — not aspirationally, but actually. The identity they are currently living from.

If they struggle with this, ask: "If I interviewed ten people who know you well, what would they say about how you show up? What would they say are your defining patterns — both the ones you're proud of and the ones you're not?"

## Reading the Collected Material

After these exercises, read back the material to the user (or have them read it themselves). Ask: "Is this accurate? Are there things here that surprise you? Are there things you expected to see but don't?"

The goal is a complete and honest portrait of the current identity — the one that Phase 4 declarations will replace, extend, or deepen.

## Key Questions

- "Which of these patterns serves you? Which costs you?"
- "If these patterns were beliefs rather than facts, when did you adopt them — and why?"
- "What are you most reluctant to look at in this inventory? That reluctance is information."
- "If you could see these patterns as a coach watching from the outside, what would you say to the person living them?"

## Completion Criteria

Phase 1 is complete when:
1. The user can name their core patterns without defensiveness — they can observe them as patterns, not facts
2. The user can articulate what their inner critic says most frequently
3. The user can distinguish at least 2-3 inherited beliefs from consciously chosen ones
4. There is no significant "unseen" material — the user has looked at the uncomfortable things, not just the comfortable ones

Do not move to Phase 2 until these criteria are met. Incomplete Phase 1 work produces declarations that avoid rather than declare.

## Pitfalls

**Intellectualizing.** The user describes patterns analytically but without any emotional contact. Gently push: "That's a description. What does it feel like to be that pattern?" The work needs to land in the body, not just the mind.

**Self-attack.** Phase 1 can tip into self-criticism rather than self-observation. If the user begins using the inventory to punish themselves, redirect: "We are not here to prosecute yourself. We are here to see. What does the observer notice?"

**Incompleteness.** Users often stop at the comfortable surface of this work. If the inventory feels too clean, ask again: "What are you not putting on this list?" The things we protect are usually the most important.

**Rushing to Phase 2.** Users often want to move quickly from seeing the uncomfortable to releasing it. Phase 2 (forgiveness) is not a way to escape Phase 1 — Phase 1 must be complete first. You cannot forgive what you haven't fully seen.

**Confusing patterns with identity.** The patterns are behaviors, not the person. Maintain this distinction carefully: "You have these patterns. You are not these patterns."
