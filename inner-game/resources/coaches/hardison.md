## Overview
Steve Hardison is known for the most expensive coaching in the world — not as a gimmick, but as a declaration of commitment. His approach centers on the radical premise that who you are *being* in any moment is more foundational than what you are doing. The Document — a co-created declaration of identity — is his signature tool.

## When to Use
- Person is stuck in endless strategy cycles while results don't change
- Person says "I know what to do, I just don't do it" — this is a BEing gap, not a DOing gap
- Person is defining themselves by circumstances, past failures, or others' opinions
- Person needs a total identity reset, not an incremental habit fix
- At major life transitions (career change, relationship shift, loss, reinvention)

## When NOT to Use
- Person needs concrete skill-building or tactical knowledge they genuinely lack
- Crisis situations requiring immediate practical action first
- Person is not ready for the level of commitment and accountability this demands
- When incremental habit change (Fogg, Clear) is the actual root need

## Core Philosophy
BEing is the cause; results are the effect. Most coaching works at the level of behavior (DOing) or outcomes (having). Hardison works at the level of identity and declaration. Language is not just descriptive — it is constitutive: declarations bring new realities into existence. The commitment device of a radical fee or a formal Document is itself a coaching move — it signals that this is not a casual conversation but a life-altering commitment.

**The Document:** A written declaration of who you *are* (not who you want to be), written in present tense, co-created with the coach. It is read aloud daily. It is not a goal list or affirmation — it is an identity statement that the person then grows into.

## Key Methods

**The Document**
- Co-created statement of identity: "I am someone who..."
- Written in present tense, not future
- Covers all domains: work, relationships, body, spirit
- Read aloud daily — the act of declaration is active, not passive
- Revisited and revised as the person grows

**BEing vs DOing Inquiry**
- Before any action, ask: "Who am I being right now?"
- Separate the person from their behavior: behavior is an expression of BEing
- When results aren't coming: look to BEing, not strategy

**Language as Creation**
- Declarations are speech acts that alter reality, not describe it
- The words you speak about yourself become the container you live in
- Identify disempowering narratives and replace with declarations

**Commitment as a Coaching Tool**
- High commitment (the fee, The Document, a public declaration) changes the game
- Low-stakes environments produce low-stakes change
- The structure of commitment is itself a transformation

**The BEing-DOing-Having Distinction**
- Most people work the chain backwards: "When I have more time/money/success, I'll do the things that let me be the person I want to be"
- Hardison reverses it: BE first → DO flows naturally → HAVE follows
- The question is never "what do I need to do?" before "who am I being?"
- Applied: if someone is procrastinating, the question is not "how do I get motivated?" but "who is the person who does not procrastinate — who are they being?"

## Key Questions
- "Who are you being right now — not what are you doing, but who are you being?"
- "If the results in your life are a perfect reflection of who you are being, what does that tell you?"
- "Who would you need to BE for these results to be inevitable?"
- "What declaration could you make right now that would change everything?"
- "Are you committed, or are you interested? Interested people do what's convenient. Committed people do what it takes."
- "What would you put in your Document — who are you, at your best, as a statement of identity?"
- "Is the story you're telling yourself about this situation true, or is it a story?"
- "What are you being that is producing the results you don't want?"
- "If you were fully the person your Document describes, what would you do in this situation?"

## Applying with a Coachee
1. Begin by surfacing the BEing-DOing confusion: "You keep talking about what to do. Let's look at who you are being first."
2. Ask who they are being when they're at their best — draw out a living example, not an abstract ideal.
3. Draft a first version of The Document together: 5-10 declarative "I am..." statements. Read them aloud. Notice which ones land and which feel hollow.
4. Assign daily reading aloud as the first commitment. Review after one week: which statements have they grown into? Which still feel aspirational?
5. Return to BEing when strategy conversations stall: the conversation about what to do is a symptom; trace it back to BEing.

## Pitfalls
- **Affirmations vs declarations:** Affirmations are wishes; declarations are commitments. Don't let the person treat The Document as a wish list.
- **Future tense leaks:** "I will be..." undermines the whole framework. Present tense only: "I am..."
- **Skipping the felt sense:** The Document must resonate emotionally, not just sound good. If it doesn't land in the body, rewrite it.
- **BEing without accountability:** Identity declarations without a structured accountability mechanism (daily reading, coaching check-ins) fade. The container matters.
- **Using it for small problems:** This method is for foundational transformation. Don't pull out a sledgehammer to hang a picture.
- **Confusing self-help with this:** The Document is not a vision board. It requires a real commitment and a coaching relationship.
