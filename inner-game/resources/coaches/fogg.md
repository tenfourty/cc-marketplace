## Overview
BJ Fogg is a Stanford researcher who cracked the actual mechanism of behavior change: behavior happens when Motivation, Ability, and a Prompt converge at the same moment. His Tiny Habits method removes the dependency on motivation (unreliable) by making behaviors so small that ability is never the constraint. The decisive insight: emotion, not repetition, creates habits.

## When to Use
- Person wants to build a lasting habit and has tried (and failed) with willpower-based approaches
- Person needs to stop an unwanted behavior and needs to understand what prompt is triggering it
- Person is overwhelmed by the size of what they want to change — Fogg starts impossibly small
- Person is highly analytical and responds to a scientific framework
- Building foundational behaviors that other goals depend on (sleep, exercise, mindfulness)

## When NOT to Use
- Person needs motivation or a purpose breakthrough first (Robbins) — Fogg assumes some baseline desire to change
- When the issue is identity-level (Hardison, Clear) rather than behavioral mechanics
- Crisis or acute emotional state — stabilize first, then design behavior
- Complex interpersonal behavior change (Goldsmith is more appropriate)

## Core Philosophy
Fogg's central challenge to conventional wisdom: you do not need more motivation, more willpower, or more discipline. Behavior change is a design problem. When behavior doesn't happen, the system is wrong — not the person. The standard advice ("just push through it") is fundamentally broken because it blames the human for a design failure. The fix is to redesign the behavior, the prompt, or the motivation — not to try harder.

**The core equation: B = MAP**
- **B**ehavior happens when **M**otivation + **A**bility + **P**rompt converge at the same moment
- If a behavior doesn't happen, one of the three elements is missing or misaligned
- Motivation fluctuates — build habits during low motivation by maximizing ability (make it tiny) and using reliable prompts

## Key Methods

**B=MAP Behavior Design**
- **Motivation:** Why does this person want this behavior? Identify the aspirations and outcomes.
- **Ability:** How easy is this behavior right now, on a scale of zero effort to enormous effort? Simplify until ability is never the barrier.
- **Prompt:** What external cue, internal state, or action trigger will reliably fire this behavior?
- Diagnose failure by identifying which element is missing: no motivation? wrong prompt? too hard?

**Tiny Habits Method**
1. Start impossibly small — if you want to floss, start with one tooth
2. Find an **Anchor Behavior** (existing habit) that naturally precedes the new habit
3. Define the recipe: "After I [anchor], I will [tiny behavior]"
4. Immediately celebrate — generate a genuine positive emotion (the "Shine")
5. Grow the habit naturally — don't force it; let motivation pull you to do more

**The Anchor-Behavior-Celebration Recipe**
- Anchor: a reliable existing behavior (brushing teeth, pouring coffee, sitting at desk)
- Behavior: the new, tiny habit (two pushups, one deep breath, write one sentence)
- Celebration: an immediate, genuine positive feeling (a word, gesture, or mental image that creates authentic positive emotion)
- This sequence is the minimal viable habit loop

**Celebration and the "Shine"**
- Fogg's most distinctive insight: emotions — not repetition — wire habits into the brain
- If you feel good immediately after a behavior, you are more likely to repeat it
- Celebration must be genuine (not performative): it creates the emotional wire
- Design a personal celebration that actually produces a positive feeling for you
- "Shine" = the feeling you want to create in the moment after the behavior

**Behavior Crafting**
- Generate a large list of possible behaviors that would achieve your aspiration
- Sort by impact (how much does this contribute to the goal?) and feasibility (how easy is this today?)
- Focus on the "Golden Behaviors" — high impact AND high feasibility
- Discard behaviors that are high impact but low feasibility for now (motivation mismatch)

**Pearl Habits (for unwanted behaviors)**
- Unwanted behaviors are responses to prompts and are meeting some need (like pearls forming around irritants)
- Identify the prompt that triggers the unwanted behavior
- Design a better behavior to do in response to the same prompt
- The old behavior's prompt becomes the anchor for the new behavior

**Starter Steps**
- When even a tiny habit feels too hard, back up to the starter step: just opening the running app, just putting on workout clothes
- The starter step changes your context and lowers the activation energy for the next behavior

## Key Questions
- "On a scale of zero to ten, how motivated are you to do this behavior? And how easy is it? Which is the limiting factor?"
- "What already happens reliably in your day that could serve as an anchor for this new behavior?"
- "What is the tiniest version of this behavior that still counts?"
- "After you do the behavior, what would a genuine moment of celebration look like for you — not performative, actually felt?"
- "If this habit has been failing, which element is missing — the prompt isn't firing, the motivation isn't there, or it's too hard?"
- "What prompt is triggering the behavior you want to stop? What could you do instead at that moment?"
- "What are all the behaviors that could contribute to this aspiration? Which ones are both high-impact AND easy enough to do today?"

## Pitfalls
- **Starting too big:** The single most common mistake. Two pushups feels embarrassing; that's the point. Resist the urge to size up immediately.
- **Forcing growth:** Fogg says let motivation pull behavior growth, don't push it. Pressure turns habits into chores.
- **Fake celebration:** A perfunctory "good job" doesn't wire the habit. The emotion must be real. Experiment until something actually generates a positive feeling.
- **Ignoring the prompt:** Most habit failures are prompt failures — the behavior is fine but there's no reliable trigger. Design the prompt as carefully as the behavior.
- **Motivation as the plan:** Planning to do the habit when you're motivated is not a plan. Design for low-motivation days.
- **Treating this as a motivation framework:** B=MAP is a design framework. If motivation is the root issue, address that separately (Robbins) first.
