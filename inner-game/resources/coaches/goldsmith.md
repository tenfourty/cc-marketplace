## Overview
Marshall Goldsmith specializes in a narrow but critical problem: why successful people self-sabotage through interpersonal habits they don't recognize, and what to do about it. His approach is stakeholder-centered, not self-centered — change is validated by how others experience you, not by how you feel. Feedforward replaces feedback because the past is fixed; the future is not.

## When to Use
- Person is highly successful but has received feedback that a specific behavior is holding them or others back
- Person struggles with interpersonal habits: interrupting, needing to add their opinion, claiming credit, failing to acknowledge others, expressing anger reactively
- Person wants to be a better leader, parent, or partner and needs behavioral change, not a mindset overhaul
- Person's environment (triggers) is driving behavior they regret
- Person wants to install a daily accountability habit (Daily Questions)

## When NOT to Use
- Person needs deep emotional processing or identity work (Brown, Hardison)
- Person lacks insight into their own behavior — they need feedback literacy first
- Person is in the wrong role entirely (a strategy problem, not a behavior problem)
- When the behavior is driven by grief, trauma, or acute mental health — refer out

## Core Philosophy
Goldsmith's key insight: the skills and behaviors that made you successful at one level can actively undermine you at the next. What got you here — drive, strong opinions, high standards, willingness to win arguments — won't get you there. Successful people need a different kind of coaching: not motivation (they have that) but behavioral awareness and stakeholder accountability.

**Triggers:** The environment constantly shapes behavior in ways we don't notice. Identifying triggers — the specific situations, people, or moments that pull out our worst habits — is the first step to changing them.

**Stakeholder-Centered Coaching:** You are not the authority on your own change. The people around you are. Their perception is the measure. This is uncomfortable but precise.

## Key Methods

**Feedforward (not Feedback)**
- Feedback is about the past — it cannot be changed, and it triggers defensiveness
- Feedforward: ask people for 2 specific suggestions for the future ("What could I do differently going forward to be a better [leader/partner/colleague]?")
- Rules: listen without comment, say only "thank you," no defending or explaining
- Repeat with multiple people — patterns emerge quickly
- Powerful because it is forward-looking, non-threatening, and generates concrete ideas

**The 20 Habits (Interpersonal)**
Common patterns in successful people that derail them:
- Winning too much (needing to win even unimportant arguments)
- Adding too much value ("Yes, and here's what I'd add...")
- Making destructive comments ("That'll never work")
- Starting sentences with "no," "but," or "however"
- Telling the world how smart you are
- Failing to give recognition
- Claiming credit you don't deserve
- Making excuses
- Not listening (checking phone, finishing sentences)
- The goal habit self-awareness brings is recognition in real time

**Daily Questions**
- Goldsmith's accountability engine: a set of active questions reviewed daily, often with a coach or accountability partner
- Structure: "Did I do my best to [behavior]?" — not "Did I [achieve outcome]?"
- Active framing: "Did I do my best to..." forces self-directed responsibility rather than passive attribution
- Example questions:
  - "Did I do my best to be happy today?"
  - "Did I do my best to find meaning?"
  - "Did I do my best to build positive relationships?"
  - "Did I do my best to make progress on [specific behavior change]?"
- Scored 1-10; tracked over time; reviewed weekly
- The act of daily review changes behavior more than any insight

**Trigger Mapping**
- Identify the specific situation, person, or environment that pulls out the undesired behavior
- STOP → PREPARE → RESPOND (instead of react)
- Create an "if/then" implementation intention: "If [trigger], then I will [chosen response]"
- Plan for the trigger before it arrives — in-the-moment willpower is unreliable

**Stakeholder Interviews**
- Structured conversations with 5-10 stakeholders: "What should [person] do more of? Less of? Keep doing?"
- Coach synthesizes themes without attribution
- Creates a behavioral change contract: 1-3 specific behaviors to change, measured over 18 months
- Monthly check-ins with stakeholders: "How am I doing on X?"

## Key Questions
- "What behavior in yourself is costing you the most in your relationships right now?"
- "What would the people closest to you say is your most annoying habit? Are they right?"
- "If you asked 5 people for feedforward on one thing, what would you ask? What are you afraid they'd say?"
- "What is the trigger? Specifically — which person, time of day, or situation reliably pulls out this behavior?"
- "Did I do my best today to [the specific thing you're trying to change]?"
- "What would you have to stop doing to become the leader you want to be?"
- "If you listened more and added your opinion less in the next 30 days, what would change?"

## Pitfalls
- **Defending during feedforward:** The rule is total silence except "thank you." Explaining or defending poisons the data and the relationship.
- **Insight without accountability:** Goldsmith is explicit: insight alone does not change behavior. The Daily Questions and stakeholder loop are non-negotiable.
- **Picking too many habits:** Focus on 1-3 maximum. Changing interpersonal habits is harder than forming new task habits.
- **Stakeholder fatigue:** Monthly check-ins must be brief and genuine. If they feel like a burden, they stop happening.
- **Applying to motivation problems:** Goldsmith is for behavioral change in already-motivated people. Don't use it when the root issue is disengagement or wrong-fit.
- **Daily Questions in passive form:** "Did I [verb]?" attributes outcome to circumstance. "Did I do my best to [verb]?" is the correct active form — it matters.
