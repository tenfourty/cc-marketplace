## Overview
Relationships covers the full spectrum of human connection — intimate partnership, family bonds, close friendships, and broader community belonging. It is consistently the domain people report matters most in retrospect, yet one of the first sacrificed under work pressure. Quality of connection is one of the strongest predictors of life satisfaction and longevity.

## Key Dimensions
- **Intimate/romantic** — depth of connection, communication quality, shared vision
- **Family** — current relationships (not just history), investment, boundaries
- **Close friendships** — the few relationships with genuine mutual knowing
- **Social network** — broader connections that provide belonging and variety
- **Community belonging** — sense of mattering to something beyond immediate circle
- **Relationship with self** — the quality of one's own company; inner relationship

## Assessment Questions
- Who in your life do you feel truly known by — not your accomplishments, but you?
- When did you last have a conversation with someone where time disappeared?
- Which relationship in your life would most benefit from more of your attention right now?
- Is there a relationship you are avoiding? What would it take to re-engage?
- How comfortable are you asking for help, or admitting vulnerability to someone close to you?
- Are your relationships net energising or net draining — and what does that ratio tell you?
- What kind of friend, partner, or family member do you most want to be — and how close are you living to that?

## Common Patterns
- **Over-investing in work relationships at the expense of personal ones** — colleagues see more of us than anyone we love. Easy to mistake professional connection for genuine intimacy.
- **Letting friendships drift** — adult friendships require deliberate maintenance. Without it, they quietly disappear. People underestimate how much they'll miss them.
- **Avoiding vulnerability** — presenting a curated version of self to protect against rejection. Results in connection that feels hollow even when plentiful.
- **Confusing activity with intimacy** — eating dinner together, attending events, texting. Presence without genuine contact.
- **Neglected partnerships** — intimate relationships treated as stable background infrastructure, starved of the investment they need to thrive.
- **Relationship debt** — unaddressed conflicts and resentments accumulate silently, then erupt or quietly kill connection.

## Quick Wins
- Reach out to one person you've been meaning to contact — this week, not "soon"
- Schedule one protected social event in the next two weeks and treat it like a critical meeting
- Try asking someone "how are you really?" and waiting for the real answer
- Put your phone away during one meal or conversation per day — fully present
- Write a brief note of appreciation to someone who has mattered to you

## Deep Work
- **Relationship audit** — map your ten most significant relationships. For each: current investment (hours/month), desired investment, energy direction (giving/receiving). Where are the gaps?
- **Attachment patterns** — explore your attachment style (anxious, avoidant, secure) and how it shows up in close relationships. *Attached* by Levine and Heller is a practical starting point.
- **Vulnerability practice** — Brené Brown's *Daring Greatly* or *The Gifts of Imperfection* as frameworks. The coaching question: what is one true thing you haven't said to someone who matters to you?
- **Identify your relational needs** — what does connection actually require for you to feel it? Many people have never named this explicitly.
- **Read:** *The Good Life* by Waldinger and Schulz (Harvard study on adult development); *Nonviolent Communication* by Marshall Rosenberg

## Pitfalls
- **Coaching the absent party** — avoid extended analysis of what a partner, parent, or colleague is doing wrong. Keep focus on what the coachee can control.
- **Endorsing avoidance** — when someone says a difficult conversation "isn't worth it," explore whether it's avoidance in disguise.
- **Treating all relationships as fixable** — some relationships are genuinely harmful. Recognise when the work is about healthy separation rather than reconnection.
- **Underestimating grief in this domain** — estranged family, ended friendships, and loneliness carry real grief. Name it before trying to fix it.
