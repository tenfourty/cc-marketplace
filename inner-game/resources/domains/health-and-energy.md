## Overview
Health and energy is the foundation domain — without physical vitality, performance in every other life area degrades. This domain covers not just absence of illness but the presence of genuine energy: the capacity to show up fully for work, relationships, and what matters. Energy is the resource that underlies all other resources. Most people only address this domain reactively, when something breaks. The coaching goal is to make it proactive.

## Key Dimensions
- **Sleep quality** — duration, consistency, and waking feeling rested (not just hours logged)
- **Physical activity** — frequency, intensity, variety, and whether movement is enjoyed or endured
- **Nutrition** — food quality, eating patterns, relationship with food (fuelling vs. self-medicating)
- **Stress management** — recovery practices, stress load relative to capacity, rest as strategy
- **Energy rhythms** — identifying peak hours, ultradian rhythms, and scheduling work accordingly
- **Body relationship** — willingness to listen to body signals and respond with self-compassion

## Assessment Questions
- When did you last wake up feeling genuinely rested and energised? What was different then?
- On a typical weekday, when is your energy highest? Lowest? How closely does your schedule reflect this?
- What is your body asking for right now that you've been ignoring or overriding?
- If your body could speak, what complaint would it lead with — and how long has it been making it?
- What does "taking care of yourself" mean to you — and is that definition actually working?
- How much of your stress is genuinely unavoidable vs. self-generated through habits, choices, and commitments?
- What is the single health behaviour whose absence costs you the most across all domains?
- When was the last time you moved your body and genuinely enjoyed it — not ground through it?
- What role does food play in your emotional life beyond fuel?

## Common Patterns
- **Ignoring body signals** — treating fatigue, pain, and tension as inconveniences rather than information. The body always wins eventually; the question is whether you learn on your terms or its terms.
- **All-or-nothing exercise** — either training for a race or completely sedentary. No sustainable middle ground means the habit collapses under any disruption.
- **Using food for emotional regulation** — eating for comfort, reward, boredom, or numbing rather than hunger. Requires gentleness in coaching; shame makes it worse.
- **Sleep deprivation as identity** — "I only need five hours" as a badge of productivity. The research is unambiguous: almost no one is the exception. Chronic undersleeep compounds cognitive impairment invisibly.
- **Boom-bust energy cycles** — grinding to exhaustion, then collapsing into recovery. Treating rest as failure rather than a required input. Athletes understand this; knowledge workers often don't.
- **Treating health as future-self's problem** — "I'll sort this out when work calms down." Work doesn't calm down. The deferral is permanent unless deliberately interrupted.
- **Confusing stimulant management with energy** — running on caffeine to override fatigue rather than addressing the fatigue. Masking the signal.

## Quick Wins
- Set a non-negotiable sleep window for the next seven nights — same bedtime, no negotiation
- Add one 20-minute walk per day; after lunch is ideal for energy regulation and blood sugar
- Drink a full glass of water before coffee in the morning — dehydration is an underrated energy drain
- Identify your one highest-energy hour of the day and protect it from meetings for two weeks; use it for the hardest work
- Do a five-minute body scan at end of each workday: where is tension held, what has it been recording?
- Remove screens from the bedroom for one week and track sleep quality

## Deep Work
- **Energy audit** — track energy levels hourly for five working days (1-10 scale with a brief note on activity, food, social contact). At the end, map patterns: what reliably raises energy, what reliably depletes it? How is current schedule aligned with findings?
- **Identify energy anchors and drains** — two explicit lists: what gives energy, what takes it. Most people can do this intuitively but have never made it explicit. Look for how time allocates to each list.
- **Design a minimum viable health protocol** — what is the smallest set of non-negotiable habits that maintains baseline function? Build the floor before the ceiling. Consistency at a low bar beats periodic excellence.
- **Sleep architecture review** — examine sleep schedule, environment, and pre-sleep routine systematically before assuming a sleep problem requires a medical solution.
- **Read:** *Why We Sleep* by Matthew Walker; *The Body Keeps the Score* by Bessel van der Kolk; *Atomic Habits* by James Clear; *The Circadian Code* by Satchin Panda

## Cross-Domain Connections
- **Inner life** — emotional stress and unprocessed feelings manifest as physical symptoms. Anxiety disrupts sleep; unexpressed grief sits in the body; chronic stress suppresses immune function. See `inner-life.md`.
- **Career and purpose** — misaligned work is a chronic low-grade stressor. Burnout is often a career alignment problem wearing a health costume. The fix is upstream. See `career-and-purpose.md`.
- **Fun and adventure** — play, physical joy, sport, dance, and time in nature are health behaviours disguised as leisure. The best exercise is the kind sustained over years. See `fun-and-adventure.md`.
- **Relationships** — social isolation is as damaging to long-term health as smoking fifteen cigarettes per day (Holt-Lunstad, 2015). Connection is physiologically restorative, not just emotionally pleasant. See `relationships.md`.

## Pitfalls
- **Medicalising what is a lifestyle question** — resist the reflex to defer everything to "see a doctor." Most chronic energy problems are behavioural and reversible with habit change.
- **Optimisation before foundations** — don't coach cold plunges and HRV tracking when the person sleeps five hours and hasn't walked in a month. Fix the basics, then optimise.
- **Shaming around health failures** — this domain carries significant guilt and self-judgment. Approach with curiosity and compassion; shame is the enemy of change.
- **Ignoring mental health as health** — anxiety, depression, and chronic stress are not character issues. They are health issues. Know when to refer and don't coach past clinical territory.
- **Accepting the premise that health is low-priority** — "I don't have time for this" is a belief, not a fact. Gently challenge the framing that health is optional.
