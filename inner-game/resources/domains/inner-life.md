## Overview
The inner life domain sits beneath all others — it is the quality of relationship with oneself that determines how every other domain is experienced. This covers self-awareness, emotional intelligence, capacity for stillness, the search for meaning, and the inner dialogue that runs constantly underneath daily life. The Inner Game framework (Timothy Gallwey) names the two selves at play here: Self 1 (the inner critic, the narrator, the judge) and Self 2 (the natural, capable self that performs best when Self 1 quiets down).

## Key Dimensions
- **Self-awareness** — accuracy of self-knowledge; gap between how you see yourself and how you actually function
- **Emotional regulation** — capacity to feel fully without being hijacked by feelings
- **Mindfulness and presence** — quality of attention in ordinary moments
- **Meaning and purpose** — felt sense that life has direction and significance
- **Spiritual practice** — broadly defined: any practice that connects to something larger than the ego
- **Solitude comfort** — capacity to be alone with oneself without anxiety or escape

## Assessment Questions
- When you are alone and quiet — genuinely quiet, no phone, no input — what arises? Is that comfortable or uncomfortable?
- What is the dominant tone of your inner narrator? Is it critical, encouraging, fearful, curious?
- When you feel a difficult emotion (frustration, shame, anxiety, grief), what do you typically do with it?
- What gives your life a felt sense of meaning right now — not what should, but what actually does?
- How much of your day do you spend genuinely present versus planning, rehearsing, or reviewing?
- What belief about yourself, if it turned out to be wrong, would most change how you show up?
- When did you last sit in silence without an agenda? What was that like?

## Common Patterns
- **Treating inner life as luxury** — "I'll meditate when I'm less busy." Treating the foundation as optional add-on, then wondering why everything feels effortful.
- **Confusing busyness with purpose** — constant activity as a way to avoid the deeper question of whether the activity points anywhere meaningful. Busyness as a defence against existential discomfort.
- **Avoiding solitude and silence** — filling every moment with input. Phones, podcasts, background noise as protection against one's own thoughts. What is being avoided?
- **Self 1 dominance** — the inner critic running the show. Constant evaluation, judgment, second-guessing. Gallwey's central observation: Self 1's interference is the primary obstacle to natural performance.
- **Emotional bypassing** — using positive thinking, spiritual language, or relentless optimism to avoid genuine emotional processing. Feelings suppressed don't disappear; they redirect.
- **Identity rigidity** — strong self-concept that can't absorb new information. Growth requires some willingness to be wrong about yourself.

## Quick Wins
- Five minutes of morning stillness before any input — no phone, no news, no agenda. Just sit.
- Practice naming emotions as they arise during the day: "I notice I'm feeling anxious/frustrated/excited right now." Naming reduces intensity.
- At the end of each day, ask one question: what moment today felt most alive? What does that tell me?
- Write one uncensored page by hand — not for anyone, not toward any goal. Just what is present.

## Deep Work
- **Meditation practice** — not as performance but as regular returning to attention. Even ten minutes daily compounds significantly over months. *Waking Up* by Sam Harris and the accompanying app offer a secular, rigorous approach. Jon Kabat-Zinn's *Full Catastrophe Living* for MBSR foundations.
- **Journaling practice** — structured reflection builds self-awareness systematically. Morning pages (Julia Cameron), prompted evening review, or free-form. The medium matters less than the habit of honest internal observation.
- **Inner Game Self 1/Self 2 awareness** — Timothy Gallwey's core framework from *The Inner Game of Tennis* (and subsequent books). The practice: observe Self 1's commentary without engaging it. Trust Self 2. This is both a philosophical orientation and a trainable skill.
- **Values clarification** — not as an intellectual exercise but as lived inquiry. What do you actually move toward when no one is watching? Values revealed by behaviour, not aspiration.
- **Read:** *The Inner Game of Tennis* by Timothy Gallwey; *Man's Search for Meaning* by Viktor Frankl; *The Untethered Soul* by Michael Singer; *Emotional Intelligence* by Daniel Goleman

## Cross-Domain Connections
- **Health and energy** — emotional stress is physiological. Unprocessed inner life shows up in the body: tension, fatigue, disrupted sleep, lowered immunity. See `health-and-energy.md`.
- **Relationships** — the quality of relationship with self sets the ceiling for quality of relationship with others. Emotional regulation, vulnerability, and empathy are inner life skills. See `relationships.md`.
- **Career and purpose** — most career confusion is identity confusion. Knowing what you actually value (vs. what you've been told to value) requires inner life work. See `career-and-purpose.md`.
- **Fun and adventure** — access to genuine joy and play is an inner life signal. Persistent joylessness or inability to be present in leisure is worth exploring here. See `fun-and-adventure.md`.

## Pitfalls
- **Pathologising normal experience** — inner work does not require a clinical lens. Anxiety, sadness, and doubt are part of a full human life, not problems to be fixed.
- **Prescribing a specific practice** — meditation is not for everyone; journaling is not for everyone. Follow the coachee's own entry points.
- **Going too deep too fast** — this domain can surface significant material. Read the room. Not every conversation is ready for full inner game exploration.
- **Spiritual bypassing** — colluding with the coachee's use of spiritual language or mindfulness framing to avoid concrete, practical emotional work. Presence and action are not opposites.
