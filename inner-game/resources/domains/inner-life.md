## Overview
The inner life domain sits beneath all others — it is the quality of relationship with oneself that determines how every other domain is experienced. This covers self-awareness, emotional intelligence, capacity for stillness, the search for meaning, and the inner dialogue that runs continuously underneath daily life. The Inner Game framework (Timothy Gallwey) names the two selves at play: Self 1 (the inner critic, narrator, judge, and controller) and Self 2 (the natural, capable self that performs best when Self 1 quiets). Most coaching ultimately comes back to this domain whether it starts there or not.

## Key Dimensions
- **Self-awareness** — accuracy of self-knowledge; the gap between how you see yourself and how you actually function under pressure
- **Emotional regulation** — capacity to feel fully without being hijacked or driven by feelings; the space between stimulus and response
- **Mindfulness and presence** — quality of attention in ordinary moments; the degree of contact with actual experience rather than commentary about it
- **Meaning and purpose** — the felt sense that life has direction, significance, and is being lived rather than endured
- **Spiritual practice** — broadly defined: any regular practice that connects to something larger than ego and immediate concern
- **Solitude comfort** — capacity to be alone with oneself without anxiety, escape, or compulsive distraction
- **Self-compassion** — the quality of one's own treatment of oneself; whether failures and imperfections are met with judgment or understanding

## Assessment Questions
- When you are genuinely alone and quiet — no phone, no input, no agenda — what arises? Is that experience comfortable or uncomfortable?
- What is the dominant tone of your inner narrator most of the time — critical, encouraging, fearful, curious, comparative?
- When you feel a difficult emotion (frustration, shame, anxiety, loneliness, grief), what is your first instinct? What do you do with it?
- What gives your life a felt sense of meaning right now — not what should, not what used to, but what actually does?
- How much of your typical day do you spend genuinely present versus planning ahead, reviewing the past, or managing how you appear?
- What belief about yourself, if it turned out to be wrong, would most change how you show up in every domain?
- When did you last sit in genuine silence without an agenda? What was that experience like?
- What are you most reluctant to look at honestly about yourself — and what is that reluctance protecting?
- What would it mean to be more at home in your own company?
- If your inner critic had a voice and a name, what would it say most often — and where did it learn to say that?
- What is the quality of your self-talk on a bad day — and would you speak that way to anyone you care about?
- What three words would you use to describe your inner atmosphere right now — not your circumstances, but the weather inside?

## Common Patterns
- **Treating inner life as luxury** — "I'll meditate when I'm less busy." The foundation treated as an optional add-on, then a persistent sense that everything feels effortful without understanding why.
- **Confusing busyness with purpose** — constant activity as a way to avoid the deeper question of whether the activity points anywhere meaningful. Busyness as a socially sanctioned defence against existential discomfort. Very common among high-achievers.
- **Avoiding solitude and silence** — filling every unstructured moment with input: phones, podcasts, background noise, social media. What is being avoided is usually available in the silence.
- **Self 1 dominance** — the inner critic running the show unchallenged. Constant evaluation, second-guessing, self-monitoring, and judgment. Gallwey's central observation: Self 1's interference is the primary obstacle to natural performance, learning, and enjoyment.
- **Emotional bypassing** — using positive thinking, spiritual language, or relentless forward-momentum to avoid genuine emotional processing. Feelings suppressed don't resolve; they redirect into the body, relationships, and behaviour.
- **Identity rigidity** — a strong, defended self-concept that cannot absorb challenging new information. Growth requires some willingness to be wrong about yourself, which requires some security in yourself.
- **Meaning avoidance** — staying busy, staying practical, staying future-focused to avoid the question of whether life as currently constructed actually feels meaningful.
- **Self-awareness as self-judgment** — using the tools of inner work (journaling, reflection, therapy language) to generate more sophisticated self-criticism rather than genuine understanding. Insight weaponised against the self.
- **Seeking experience over integration** — collecting retreats, workshops, and peak experiences without integrating what they surface. The insight high without the behaviour change.

## Coaching Approach
This domain often appears inside other domain conversations — someone discusses their career and what emerges is a question of identity; someone discusses relationships and what surfaces is the quality of their relationship with themselves. Don't force the conversation to this level; but don't redirect away from it either when it naturally appears. The most useful questions are simple and concrete: "what do you notice?" and "what are you telling yourself about that?" Stay with what is present rather than reaching for frameworks too quickly.

## Quick Wins
- Five minutes of morning stillness before any input — no phone, no news, no plan. Just sit with whatever is present.
- Practice naming emotions as they arise during the day: "I notice I'm feeling anxious / frustrated / excited / envious right now." Naming reduces emotional intensity measurably and creates the pause that makes choice possible.
- At the end of each day, one question: what moment today felt most alive? What does the pattern of answers over a week reveal?
- Write one uncensored page by hand — not for anyone, not toward any goal. Only what is actually present. No editing.
- Notice your Self 1 commentary today — the running internal evaluation of your performance, other people, and circumstances. Just observe it without engaging. Name it: "there's Self 1 again."
- Identify one belief you hold about yourself that you have never questioned — and ask where it came from.

## Deep Work
- **Meditation practice** — not as performance or productivity hack but as regular, repeated practice of returning attention. Even ten undramatic minutes daily compounds significantly. *Waking Up* by Sam Harris offers a secular, rigorous entry point. Jon Kabat-Zinn's *Full Catastrophe Living* provides the scientific foundations of MBSR. The practice matters more than the system.
- **Journaling practice** — structured reflection builds self-awareness systematically over time. Morning pages (Julia Cameron), prompted evening review, or completely free-form. The medium matters less than the honesty and consistency. A journal that challenges you is more valuable than one that flatters you.
- **Inner Game Self 1/Self 2 awareness** — Timothy Gallwey's framework from *The Inner Game of Tennis* (and subsequent books on work, music, and stress). The practice: observe Self 1's commentary without engaging it; trust Self 2's capacity without coaching it. This is simultaneously a philosophical orientation and a trainable skill with immediate practical application.
- **Values clarification as lived inquiry** — not as an intellectual exercise with a worksheet but as ongoing honest observation. What do you actually move toward when no one is watching and there is no external reward? Revealed preference over stated preference.
- **Sit with a difficult feeling for five minutes without acting on it** — build the capacity to be present with discomfort rather than immediately resolving, avoiding, or reframing it. This is the foundational skill underlying emotional regulation, and it is trainable.
- **Read:** *The Inner Game of Tennis* by Timothy Gallwey; *Man's Search for Meaning* by Viktor Frankl; *The Untethered Soul* by Michael Singer; *Emotional Intelligence* by Daniel Goleman; *Waking Up* by Sam Harris

## Cross-Domain Connections
- **Health and energy** — emotional stress is physiological. Unprocessed inner life shows up in the body: tension, fatigue, disrupted sleep, lowered immunity, chronic pain. The body keeps the score whether the mind acknowledges it or not. See `health-and-energy.md`.
- **Relationships** — the quality of relationship with self sets the ceiling for quality of relationship with others. Emotional regulation, vulnerability, empathy, and genuine presence are all inner life skills expressed outward. See `relationships.md`.
- **Career and purpose** — most career confusion, at sufficient depth, is identity confusion. Knowing what you genuinely value (rather than what you've been conditioned to pursue) requires sustained inner life work. See `career-and-purpose.md`.
- **Fun and adventure** — access to genuine joy and the capacity for play is an inner life signal. Persistent inability to experience either, even when time and permission exist, is worth exploring at this level. See `fun-and-adventure.md`.

## Signs of a Healthy Score (7-10)
Someone scoring well typically: has a practice of some kind that cultivates self-awareness; can name and navigate difficult emotions without being swept away by them; spends meaningful time in genuine presence rather than constant commentary; has a felt sense that life has direction and significance; is comfortable in their own company; and generally treats themselves with the same basic respect and curiosity they'd offer a good friend.

## Pitfalls
- **Pathologising normal human experience** — inner work does not require a clinical frame. Anxiety, sadness, doubt, and inner conflict are part of a full human life. Not every inner experience is a problem to be solved.
- **Prescribing a specific practice** — meditation is not for everyone in every form; journaling is not for everyone. Follow the coachee's own natural entry points into self-reflection rather than imposing a method.
- **Going too deep too fast** — this domain can surface significant material quickly. Read the room. Not every conversation is resourced for deep inner game exploration. Build the container before going into it.
- **Spiritual bypassing** — colluding with the coachee's use of mindfulness or spiritual framing to avoid concrete, practical emotional work. Presence and action are not opposites. Insight that produces no change has limited value.
- **Collapsing to philosophy** — this domain attracts abstract conversation. Keep it personal, specific, and grounded. The question is not "what is meaning?" but "what gives your life meaning right now?"
- **Assuming this domain is only for people in crisis** — the inner life needs regular maintenance, not just emergency repair. Coaching this domain proactively is more powerful than waiting for breakdown.
