## Overview
The finances domain addresses both the practical mechanics of money and the psychological relationship with it — because behaviour follows belief. Financial stress affects decision-making, relationship quality, health, and willingness to take risks in every other domain. The goal is not wealth for its own sake but financial agency: enough freedom and security to make choices that align with values.

## Key Dimensions
- **Income sufficiency** — does income cover needs, wants, and future with margin?
- **Savings rate** — what percentage of income is being retained and deployed?
- **Debt management** — clarity on what is owed, cost of debt, and trajectory
- **Investment growth** — assets working over time; compounding as a long-term engine
- **Financial security feeling** — subjective sense of safety, independent of actual numbers
- **Money beliefs** — the inherited and formed beliefs driving financial behaviour

## Assessment Questions
- Do you know your actual net worth, monthly burn rate, and savings rate right now? If not, what would it mean to find out?
- What feeling arises when you think about your finances — and is that feeling proportional to the reality?
- What is your earliest memory of money, and what did it teach you?
- What does "enough" mean to you — and is that number defined, or always just a bit more than you have?
- Is your spending aligned with your stated values, or is there a gap?
- If your income dropped by thirty percent, what would you do — and how does your answer inform what you should be doing now?
- What financial conversation have you been avoiding, with yourself or someone else?

## Common Patterns
- **Avoidance** — not looking at accounts, deferring financial decisions, vague anxiety without clarity. "I'll deal with it later" as a long-term strategy.
- **Scarcity mindset despite objective abundance** — spending anxiety and financial stress at income levels that objectively support security. The feeling is real; the threat is not.
- **Lifestyle inflation** — income grows, spending grows to match, security never improves. The hedonic treadmill in financial form.
- **Inherited money beliefs from parents** — beliefs about money being dangerous, shameful, scarce, or corrupting. Often operate entirely below conscious awareness.
- **Conflating net worth with self-worth** — financial success becomes identity, making honest financial assessment threatening.
- **Short-termism** — optimising for current comfort at the expense of future options. The present bias is powerful and universal.

## Quick Wins
- Know your three numbers: net worth, monthly burn, savings rate. Calculate them this week if you don't know them.
- Set up one automatic transfer to savings — remove the decision from willpower
- Review last month's spending against your stated priorities. Where is the largest gap?
- List every recurring subscription or payment. Cancel one that no longer serves you.

## Deep Work
- **Money autobiography** — write the story of your relationship with money from childhood to now. Include: your family's relationship with money, the first time you felt financially anxious or secure, the beliefs you formed, the decisions those beliefs drove. This is foundational to changing behaviour.
- **Inherited beliefs examination** — identify three beliefs about money you hold as true. For each: where did it come from? Is it actually true? Is it serving you?
- **Define "enough"** — a specific, grounded number and lifestyle picture. What does financial freedom actually look like for you? Not a vague "more" but a concrete threshold.
- **Values-spending alignment audit** — categorise three months of spending. Compare allocation to stated top five values. Reckon with the gap honestly.
- **Read:** *The Psychology of Money* by Morgan Housel; *Your Money or Your Life* by Vicki Robin; *Die With Zero* by Bill Perkins (provocative counterpoint)

## Pitfalls
- **Playing financial advisor** — the coaching role is on beliefs, behaviour, and clarity. Specific investment or tax advice is not the coach's domain.
- **Assuming financial stress is about money** — often it's about control, security, identity, or relationship. Dig before assuming.
- **Avoiding the numbers conversation** — some coaches collude with avoidance. Gently insist on knowing the actual situation.
- **Moralising** — money choices carry cultural and moral weight. Stay curious rather than evaluative about spending patterns.
