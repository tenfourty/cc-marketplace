## Overview
The finances domain addresses both the practical mechanics of money and the psychological relationship with it — because financial behaviour follows financial belief. The numbers matter, but the feelings, stories, and inherited beliefs about money matter more in determining why people do or don't manage it well. Financial stress affects decision-making quality, relationship dynamics, health, and the willingness to take risks in every other domain. The goal is not wealth as an end but financial agency: enough freedom and security to make choices aligned with values.

## Key Dimensions
- **Income sufficiency** — does current income cover needs, meaningful wants, and future with genuine margin?
- **Savings rate** — what percentage of income is being retained, and is it being deployed intentionally?
- **Debt management** — clarity on what is owed, the real cost of that debt, and whether the trajectory is improving
- **Investment growth** — assets working over time; the compounding engine for long-term options
- **Financial security feeling** — the subjective felt sense of safety, which often diverges significantly from objective numbers
- **Money beliefs** — the inherited and formed beliefs driving financial behaviour, often invisible and pre-rational
- **Financial agency** — the degree to which financial circumstances enable or constrain meaningful life choices; the experience of options vs. constraints

## Assessment Questions
- Do you know your actual net worth, monthly burn rate, and savings rate right now? If not, what feeling arises when you consider finding out?
- What emotion comes up most reliably when you think about your finances — anxiety, shame, avoidance, curiosity, control?
- What is your earliest clear memory involving money, and what did it teach you — explicitly or implicitly?
- What does "enough" mean to you — and is that a specific, defined number and lifestyle, or always slightly more than you currently have?
- If you mapped your spending against your stated values for the last three months, what would the gap look like?
- If your income dropped by thirty percent tomorrow, what would you do — and does your answer reveal anything you should be doing now?
- What financial conversation have you been avoiding — with yourself, a partner, or someone you owe or are owed by?
- What financial decisions are you making based on fear rather than reasoned choice?
- What would financial freedom actually allow you to do that you can't do now?
- Is your current relationship with money something you would want your children to inherit? What would you want to pass on instead?
- What is the most financially courageous thing you could do right now — and what is actually stopping you?
- What does "financial stress" feel like in your body, and how does it affect your behaviour and decision-making in other areas?

## Common Patterns
- **Avoidance** — not opening statements, deferring financial decisions, carrying vague ambient anxiety without ever getting specific. "I'll deal with it when I have more time" deployed indefinitely.
- **Scarcity mindset despite objective abundance** — spending anxiety and chronic financial stress at income levels that objectively support security. The feeling is real and valid; the threat driving it may not be proportional.
- **Lifestyle inflation** — income grows, spending grows to match, actual security never improves. The hedonic treadmill running in financial form. Each raise absorbed without changing the trajectory.
- **Inherited money beliefs operating below awareness** — beliefs about money being dangerous, shameful, scarce, or morally corrupting. Often originate in family of origin and run the financial show without being examined.
- **Conflating net worth with self-worth** — financial success becomes core identity, making honest financial assessment threatening. Losses hit harder than equivalent gains feel good.
- **Short-termism** — systematically optimising for current comfort at the expense of future optionality. The present bias is universal and powerful; it requires deliberate counteraction.
- **Magical thinking about future income** — "when I'm earning more, I'll start saving." The savings habit is usually independent of income level. It starts now or it doesn't start.
- **Using spending as emotional management** — retail therapy, reward purchases, lifestyle signalling. Spending as a response to emotions that haven't been examined. Closely related to the food-as-emotional-regulation pattern in the health domain.

## Coaching Approach
The most useful early intervention in this domain is making the vague specific: knowing the actual numbers eliminates the imagination-driven anxiety that avoidance creates. Approach the first "know your numbers" conversation with curiosity rather than urgency. Most people are relieved once they actually look. From there, the work splits into two tracks: practical (what to do) and psychological (why behaviour doesn't match knowledge). Both tracks are usually necessary.

## Quick Wins
- Know your three numbers this week: net worth (assets minus liabilities), monthly burn rate, current savings rate. Calculate them if you don't already know them.
- Set up one automatic transfer to savings — remove the decision from willpower entirely; make the behaviour the default
- Review last month's spending against your top five stated priorities. Name the largest gap honestly.
- List every recurring subscription or automated payment. Cancel one that no longer reflects a genuine choice.
- Have one honest financial conversation you've been deferring — with yourself, a partner, or a relevant party
- Write down what "financial freedom" would actually look like in concrete daily terms — not the number, but the life it enables
- Identify one money decision you've been avoiding and schedule a specific time this week to address it

## Deep Work
- **Money autobiography** — write the story of your relationship with money from childhood to now. Cover: your family's explicit and implicit relationship with money, the first time you felt financially anxious or secure, the beliefs you formed about what money means, the decisions those beliefs have driven. This narrative work is foundational to changing behaviour, because behaviour follows story.
- **Inherited beliefs examination** — identify three beliefs about money you hold as true and act on. For each: trace where it came from. Examine whether it is actually true as a general principle. Assess whether it is currently serving you or costing you.
- **Define "enough" specifically** — a concrete, grounded number and lifestyle picture. What does financial freedom actually look like for you — not a vague "more security" but a specific threshold? Many people have never defined this, which means the goal is always receding.
- **Values-spending alignment audit** — categorise three months of actual spending. Compare the allocation across categories against your stated top five values. Sit with the gap honestly before trying to fix it.
- **Long-term compounding visualisation** — model what the current savings rate produces over ten and twenty years. Seeing the actual numbers often creates more motivation than any argument.
- **Read:** *The Psychology of Money* by Morgan Housel; *Your Money or Your Life* by Vicki Robin; *The Millionaire Next Door* by Stanley and Danko; *Die With Zero* by Bill Perkins (useful as a provocative counterpoint)

## Cross-Domain Connections
- **Career and purpose** — financial clarity creates career courage. Knowing your actual "enough" number enables the pivot, the negotiation, and the risk that financial vagueness prevents. See `career-and-purpose.md`.
- **Inner life** — money beliefs are a subset of core beliefs about self, safety, and worth. Coaching financial behaviour without coaching the underlying beliefs rarely produces lasting change. See `inner-life.md`.
- **Relationships** — money is among the most reliable sources of relationship conflict and avoidance. Shared financial vision — or its absence — shapes partnership dynamics in ways that are hard to overstate. See `relationships.md`.
- **Growth and learning** — financial literacy is learnable. Many people treat financial ignorance as a fixed characteristic rather than a skill gap that can be closed. See `growth-and-learning.md`.

## Signs of a Healthy Score (7-10)
Someone scoring well typically: knows their key numbers without having to look them up; has a savings rate they feel good about; has no debt they are avoiding thinking about; feels genuine security rather than ambient anxiety about money; spends in rough alignment with their values; and has a defined picture of what "enough" looks like rather than an ever-receding target.

## Pitfalls
- **Playing financial advisor** — the coaching role addresses beliefs, behaviour, and emotional relationship with money. Specific investment strategy, tax advice, or product recommendations are not the coach's domain.
- **Assuming financial stress is primarily about money** — often it is about control, identity, security, childhood experience, or relationship. Dig before assuming the surface issue is the real one.
- **Avoiding the numbers conversation** — some coaches collude with avoidance by staying in feelings territory and never asking what the actual numbers are. Gentle insistence on knowing the real situation is part of the work.
- **Moralising** — spending choices carry cultural and moral freight. Stay curious and non-evaluative about patterns before introducing any evaluative frame.
- **Underestimating how personal money history is** — financial behaviour is rarely rational and rarely shallow. Treat money stories with the same respect as any other formative experience.
- **Stopping at behaviour change** — automating savings and cutting subscriptions are useful but surface interventions. The deeper work — on beliefs, stories, and inherited patterns — is what produces lasting change.
