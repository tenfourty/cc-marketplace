## Overview
Career and purpose goes deeper than professional success — it asks whether the work you do aligns with who you are and what you're here to contribute. High scores on conventional career metrics (title, income, influence) can coexist with profound dissatisfaction when mission alignment is absent. This domain explores both the external shape of a career and the internal experience of doing it. The distinction between a satisfying career and a meaningful one is often the central question in this domain.

## Key Dimensions
- **Daily enjoyment** — how work actually feels most of the time, not just in highlight moments or after big wins
- **Mission alignment** — the connection between daily work and something that genuinely matters to you
- **Growth trajectory** — whether you are actively developing, plateauing, or experiencing gradual decline
- **Impact and contribution** — the degree to which you can see the difference your work makes to others
- **Autonomy** — real control over how, when, with whom, and on what you work
- **Mastery** — active pursuit of genuine excellence in something you care about
- **Energy balance** — whether the work is net energising or net depleting across the week; chronic depletion is a diagnostic signal even when all other measures look acceptable

## Assessment Questions
- On Sunday evening, what is your dominant feeling about Monday? What does that tell you?
- Which parts of your work reliably make you lose track of time? Which drain you in the first five minutes?
- If you were still in this role in five years, what would need to be true for that to feel like success rather than stagnation?
- When did you last feel that your work genuinely mattered — that something real would have been worse without your contribution?
- What would you work on if compensation were guaranteed and reputation were irrelevant?
- Is your career currently serving your life, or has your life become a support structure for your career?
- What are you avoiding figuring out about your career direction — and what has made it easier to avoid?
- What would the most courageous career move you could make right now actually be?
- What feedback have you received about your strengths that you haven't fully believed or acted on?
- What would you tell a younger version of yourself about the career decisions you've made — with full benefit of hindsight?
- Whose career do you most admire (without envying their specific circumstances) — and what does that reveal about what you actually want?
- What would you need to believe about yourself — that you don't currently believe — for the career move you most want to make to feel possible?

## Common Patterns
- **Golden handcuffs** — compensation, status, or security keeping someone in misaligned work. The trap becomes invisible over time; each year invested raises the cost of leaving.
- **Confusing career success with life satisfaction** — high achievement in a career that doesn't fit is its own form of failure. Prestigious misery is still misery.
- **Neglecting purpose for pragmatism** — "I'll do meaningful work after I've made enough." The financial threshold always moves. The meaningful work stays deferred.
- **Identity fusion** — role and self become indistinguishable. Career threat becomes existential threat. This makes honest assessment of fit nearly impossible.
- **Competence trap** — being very good at something you no longer want to do. External success in the wrong direction compounds the difficulty of changing course.
- **Waiting for permission** — waiting for someone to recognise potential and create the opportunity, rather than taking responsibility for creating it.
- **Optimising for optionality indefinitely** — staying vague and uncommitted as a strategy to keep all doors open. Often prevents depth, mastery, and meaning.
- **The "good enough" trap** — tolerating a career that is fine but not fulfilling. The absence of crisis is mistaken for the presence of satisfaction. Low-grade misalignment is easy to rationalise and hard to feel urgently enough to address.

## Coaching Approach
Resist the pull toward problem-solving and job-change advice. The most useful early inquiry is separating the situation (facts about the work) from the story (interpretation and meaning assigned to it). Many career problems dissolve with that distinction; others sharpen into clarity about what actually needs to change. Diagnosis before prescription. The energy audit on work activities (quick win) is a useful, low-stakes starting point that often surfaces the real conversation.

## Quick Wins
- Do an energy audit of work activities this week: mark each task and interaction with + (energising) or - (draining). Look at the pattern without judgment.
- Identify one task you've been holding that should be delegated or dropped — and actually do it this week
- Schedule one conversation with someone whose career trajectory or work genuinely interests you
- Block one hour this week for the most important work you've been deferring to reactive time
- Write down the three things at work that most reliably make you feel your time was well spent — examine how much of the average week goes to them
- Identify one thing that felt alive in your work five years ago that has since disappeared — what happened to it?

## Deep Work
- **Purpose exploration** — not "what is my purpose" (too abstract to be answerable) but: what problems draw my attention, who do I want to serve, what would I regret not having attempted? Viktor Frankl's *Man's Search for Meaning* and Simon Sinek's *Start With Why* as complementary frameworks.
- **Values-career alignment check** — identify your top five values (a card sort exercise is useful here). Score your current work 1-10 on each value. Name the three largest gaps. What would it take to close one of them?
- **Ikigai mapping** — the intersection of what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. Not a formula but a structured inquiry that often surfaces important tensions and overlooked possibilities.
- **Designing your ideal week** — Cal Newport's exercise: if work were structured around your genuine strengths, values, and energy rhythms, what would a week actually look like? How far is today's week from that picture, and what is one change that closes the gap?
- **Skill-to-value gap analysis** — what capabilities would make you dramatically more effective at work that matters to you? This bridges career and growth domains.
- **Read:** *So Good They Can't Ignore You* by Cal Newport; *Designing Your Life* by Burnett and Evans; *Range* by David Epstein; *Man's Search for Meaning* by Viktor Frankl

## Cross-Domain Connections
- **Finances** — career choices and financial constraints are deeply entangled. Clarity on what "enough" actually means unlocks career courage that financial vagueness prevents. See `finances.md`.
- **Growth and learning** — mastery and skill development are central to career vitality and meaning. Skill stagnation is one of the most reliable precursors to career disengagement. See `growth-and-learning.md`.
- **Inner life** — most career confusion is identity confusion underneath. Knowing what you actually value (versus what you've been conditioned to want) requires inner work. See `inner-life.md`.

## Signs of a Healthy Score (7-10)
Someone scoring well typically: looks forward to Monday more often than not; can name what they are working toward and why it matters; feels their skills are being used and stretched; sees clear evidence of their contribution; has meaningful autonomy over how they work; and experiences enough day-to-day engagement that the work feels like more than a transaction. They may have frustrations, but the underlying direction feels right.

## Pitfalls
- **Rushing to solutions** — "find a new job" is rarely the right first move. Thorough diagnosis before prescription. The same person brings the same patterns to a new role.
- **Treating all career problems as purpose problems** — sometimes it is a bad manager, a dysfunctional team, or a fixable structural constraint. Purpose inquiry can itself become avoidance of a difficult practical conversation.
- **Underestimating the real cost of transitions** — career pivots require real courage, time, financial runway, and tolerance for ambiguity. Acknowledge the weight of what is being considered.
- **Romanticising passion** — "follow your passion" as prescriptive advice can be actively harmful. Passion frequently follows mastery and contribution rather than preceding them. Cal Newport's critique is worth understanding.
- **Colluding with stuckness** — sometimes the coaching conversation becomes a holding environment for inaction. Name it gently if the exploration produces insight but never movement.
- **Missing the window** — some career problems have time-sensitive solutions (an internal move, a conversation, a pivot while still young enough). Don't over-process when decisive action is available.
- **Treating this as a one-time inquiry** — career and purpose alignment requires periodic recalibration as life circumstances, values, and ambitions evolve. What was right five years ago may not be right now.
