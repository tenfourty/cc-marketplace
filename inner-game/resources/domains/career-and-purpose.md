## Overview
Career and purpose goes deeper than professional success — it asks whether the work you do aligns with who you are and what you're here to contribute. High scores on conventional career metrics (title, income, influence) can coexist with profound dissatisfaction when mission alignment is absent. This domain explores both the external shape of a career and the internal experience of doing it.

## Key Dimensions
- **Daily enjoyment** — how work feels most of the time, not in highlight moments
- **Mission alignment** — connection between daily work and something that genuinely matters to you
- **Growth trajectory** — are you developing, plateauing, or declining?
- **Impact and contribution** — seeing the difference your work makes to others
- **Autonomy** — control over how, when, and with whom you work
- **Mastery** — pursuit of genuine excellence in something meaningful

## Assessment Questions
- On Sunday evening, what is your dominant feeling about Monday? What does that tell you?
- Which parts of your work make you lose track of time? Which parts drain you in the first five minutes?
- If you were doing this work in five years, what would need to be true for that to feel like success?
- When did you last feel that your work genuinely mattered — that something would have been worse without you?
- What would you work on if money were not a factor and reputation were irrelevant?
- Is your career serving your life, or has your life become a support structure for your career?
- What are you avoiding figuring out about your career direction?

## Common Patterns
- **Golden handcuffs** — compensation, status, or security keeping someone in misaligned work. The trap becomes invisible over time.
- **Confusing career success with life satisfaction** — high achievement in a career that doesn't fit is its own kind of failure.
- **Neglecting purpose for pragmatism** — "I'll do meaningful work after I've made enough money." The threshold always moves.
- **Identity fusion** — role and self become indistinguishable. Career threat becomes existential threat.
- **Competence trap** — being very good at something you no longer want to do. Success in the wrong direction feels like failure.
- **Waiting for permission** — waiting for someone to recognise potential and create the opportunity, rather than creating it.

## Quick Wins
- Do an energy audit on work activities this week: mark each task + (energising) or - (draining). Look at the pattern.
- Identify one task you've been holding that should be delegated or dropped — and actually do it
- Schedule one conversation with someone whose career trajectory genuinely interests you
- Block one hour this week for work that matters most, before the day fills with reactive tasks

## Deep Work
- **Purpose exploration** — not "what is my purpose" (too abstract) but: what problems am I drawn to, who do I want to help, what would I regret not having tried? See Simon Sinek's *Start With Why* and Viktor Frankl's *Man's Search for Meaning*.
- **Values-career alignment check** — list your top five values (from a values card sort or similar). Score your current work 1-10 on each. Where are the gaps? What would close them?
- **Ikigai mapping** — the Japanese framework: intersection of what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, what you can be paid for. Not a formula but a useful inquiry.
- **Designing your ideal week** — Cal Newport's exercise: what would a week look like if work were structured around your strengths and values? How far is that from today?
- **Read:** *So Good They Can't Ignore You* by Cal Newport; *Designing Your Life* by Burnett and Evans; *Range* by David Epstein

## Pitfalls
- **Rushing to solutions** — "find a new job" is rarely the right first move. Understand the diagnosis before prescribing.
- **Treating all career problems as purpose problems** — sometimes it's a bad manager, a dysfunctional team, or a fixable structural issue. Purpose inquiry can become avoidance of a practical conversation.
- **Underestimating transition costs** — career pivots are real and hard. Don't minimise the courage and risk they require.
- **Romanticising passion** — the "follow your passion" framework can be actively harmful. Passion often follows mastery and contribution, not the other way around.
