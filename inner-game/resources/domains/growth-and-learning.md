## Overview
Growth and learning is the domain of becoming — it addresses the human drive to develop, expand, and compound capability over time. It covers not just formal education but the full ecology of how someone takes in new ideas, builds skills, and — critically — applies what they learn. Without deliberate learning, capability plateaus and work gradually narrows.

## Key Dimensions
- **Current learning projects** — what is actively being studied or developed right now?
- **Skill development** — deliberate practice in areas that matter; closing identified gaps
- **Reading and input quality** — the diet of ideas feeding thinking and perspective
- **Mental model diversity** — breadth of frameworks for understanding the world
- **Curiosity level** — intrinsic drive to explore, question, and understand
- **Application rate** — the ratio of learning to doing; knowledge becoming capability

## Assessment Questions
- What are you learning right now that genuinely excites you? If nothing comes to mind, when did that change?
- What skill, if you developed it significantly over the next year, would most change your trajectory?
- What is the most important thing you've read or learned in the past six months — and how has it changed what you do?
- Is your learning largely within your existing domain, or does it regularly pull you into unfamiliar territory?
- Are you learning at the edge of your comfort zone, or staying in territory you already know?
- What have you learned recently that you've actually applied — and what sits unimplemented?
- Who in your life challenges your thinking? How much time do you spend with them?

## Common Patterns
- **Consumption without application** — reading, podcasts, courses as intellectual entertainment rather than capability building. The shelf of unread books; the started course; the highlights never revisited.
- **Learning as procrastination** — "I need to learn more before I start." Preparation that never ends because the real resistance is to starting.
- **Narrow expertise** — deep competence in one area with atrophied curiosity outside it. Specialist tunnel vision that limits both creativity and career options.
- **Credential-seeking over capability-building** — pursuing qualifications for legitimacy rather than learning for genuine development.
- **Passive consumption mistaken for learning** — reading without annotation, listening without reflection, watching without application. Feels productive; largely isn't.

## Quick Wins
- Read ten pages of a genuinely challenging book today — not industry content, something that stretches you
- Teach one thing you learned recently to someone else (explaining forces real understanding)
- Identify the one skill gap most limiting your effectiveness right now and name it specifically
- Listen to a podcast or read something completely outside your field this week — cross-pollination is a creativity multiplier

## Deep Work
- **Identify your learning style** — not the debunked VAK model but: how do you actually retain and apply? Through teaching others, writing, building, discussing, experimenting? Design your learning around how you work, not how you think you should work.
- **Build a personal curriculum** — given where you want to be in three years, what do you need to know and be able to do that you can't now? Work backwards into a learning plan with actual time allocations.
- **Expertise expansion** — deliberately study one field adjacent to your own per quarter. David Epstein's *Range* documents the outsized advantage of cross-domain thinking.
- **Close the application loop** — for every major input (book, course, workshop), write down three specific changes to behaviour or practice before moving on. No completion without commitment.
- **Read:** *Range* by David Epstein; *Ultralearning* by Scott Young; *A Mind for Numbers* by Barbara Oakley; *The Art of Learning* by Josh Waitzkin

## Cross-Domain Connections
- **Career and purpose** — mastery is a source of meaning and engagement. Skill stagnation is one of the leading causes of career disengagement. See `career-and-purpose.md`.
- **Fun and adventure** — genuine curiosity and play are the same impulse. Learning new things for the joy of it, not for utility, restores intrinsic motivation. See `fun-and-adventure.md`.
- **Inner life** — intellectual humility — knowing how much you don't know — is a self-awareness practice. Good learners have developed tolerance for not-knowing. See `inner-life.md`.
- **Finances** — financial literacy is a learnable skill, not a personality trait. The gap is usually knowledge and habit, not intelligence. See `finances.md`.

## Pitfalls
- **Prescribing a reading list** — follow curiosity, not a coaching agenda. The coachee's own learning direction is more sustainable than a recommended curriculum.
- **Conflating intelligence with learning habits** — smart people can have terrible learning practices. Separate the two.
- **Treating busyness as a barrier** — "I don't have time to learn" usually means "I haven't made it a priority." Probe gently.
- **Ignoring the application gap** — it is easy to celebrate the learning and skip the harder question of what has actually changed as a result.
