## Overview

Messages that reinforce company values, acknowledge the human side of work, and demonstrate care for people's well-being. Empathetic, vulnerable, and values-driven. These are the messages that define culture.

## Key Characteristics

- **Acknowledges reality:** Doesn't sugarcoat difficult situations or burnout.
- **Gives permission:** Explicitly tells people it's OK to take breaks, ask for help, or slow down.
- **Expresses gratitude:** Thanks people for their effort and resilience.
- **Balances push and care:** Maintains high standards while showing genuine concern.
- **Vulnerable leadership:** Shares own struggles or needs ("I need a break too").
- **Values-driven:** Connects actions to company values.

## Structure Template

```
[Channel], [Emoji] [Title — often a weekly reflection or personal note]

[Acknowledgement of reality — what people are feeling]

[Permission-giving — it's OK to take a break / ask for help]

[Gratitude — specific things you're thankful for]

[Reframe — connect difficulty to purpose or potential]

[Practical next step — take time off, come talk to me, etc.]
```

### Example A: Acknowledging Burnout and Giving Permission

> I can feel the fatigue across the team — we've been sprinting for weeks and it shows. I want to be direct: please take your time off. Don't postpone it, don't feel guilty about it.
>
> That said, sustainable pace isn't just about breaks. It's about working smarter — automating the repetitive stuff, cutting meetings that don't earn their time, saying no to the things that don't move the needle.
>
> I'm grateful for the effort everyone's putting in. But effort without rest leads to burnout, not results. Look after yourselves.

**Why it works:** Names the reality without sugarcoating. Gives explicit permission. Balances care with practical advice. Ends with genuine concern.

### Example B: Weekly Reflection with Vulnerability

> #team, :coffee: End of week thoughts
>
> Honest moment: this has been a tough quarter. We lost two great people, shipped under pressure, and navigated a re-org that none of us asked for.
>
> I'm taking a few days off next week and I'd encourage you to do the same if you can. It genuinely helps.
>
> I know the mood isn't great right now. I want to acknowledge that without pretending otherwise. But I also want to say — I'm genuinely optimistic about where we're headed. The team we have today is strong, and the product is in a better place than it was six months ago.

**Why it works:** Models the behaviour (taking time off). Names the hard truths honestly. Validates feelings without dismissing them. Ends with grounded optimism.

### Example C: Gratitude and Specific Thanks

> As we wrap up the quarter, a few things I'm grateful for:
> * The way the on-call team handled last week's incident — calm, coordinated, zero finger-pointing
> * How quickly the new hires have got up to speed. That's a reflection of the team culture, not just good onboarding docs
> * The fact that we delivered everything on the roadmap despite being down two engineers. That took real collaboration across teams.
> * Honestly? I'm grateful for the upcoming bank holiday. I need the reset and I suspect I'm not alone :slightly_smiling_face:

**Why it works:** Specific gratitude (not generic). Vulnerable ("I need the reset"). Acknowledges both what was achieved and how it was achieved.

## Common Mistakes

- Being preachy about self-care without modelling it yourself
- Sugarcoating — people see through it and trust erodes
- Generic gratitude ("thanks team!" without specifics)
- Only sending these when things are bad (send them when things are good too)
- Forgetting the practical next step (take time off, come talk to me)

## PCM Variations

- **Thinker:** Include specific data points about what was achieved
- **Persister:** Connect to mission and long-term purpose
- **Harmoniser:** Extra warmth, personal touch, "my door is always open"
- **General audience:** Default template — balance acknowledgement, permission, gratitude, and purpose
