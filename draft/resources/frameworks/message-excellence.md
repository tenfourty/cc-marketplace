## Purpose

A comprehensive framework for reviewing and improving written messages. Use this to evaluate drafts before presenting them to the user. The goal: make communication so clear that recipients can quickly understand, decide, and act without needing clarification.

## Principle 1: Clear Thinking Before Clear Writing

The real bottleneck in communication is not word choice — it's clarity of thought. Before reviewing surface-level writing, check that the draft has:

1. **A clear perspective** — what is the core message?
2. **A decision about importance** — what matters most to the reader?
3. **Organised hierarchy** — main point, supporting points, context
4. **An articulated "so what"** — why should the reader care?

If the thinking is unclear, no amount of editing will fix the message. Flag it.

## Principle 2: Main Point Above, Context Below

Every message must lead with the point. This is non-negotiable.

**The 2-3 Sentence Rule:** The first 2-3 sentences should contain:
- The key message or request
- What the sender needs from the recipient
- Why it matters to the reader

Everything else — background, reasoning, detail — goes below.

**Anti-pattern: Chronological storytelling.**
People naturally write in the order they experienced events: "First this happened, then this, then I noticed..." This buries the point. Always restructure to lead with the conclusion.

**Test:** If someone reads only the first 3 sentences, do they know what this message is about and what's expected of them?

## Principle 3: Concise Means Dense, Not Short

Being concise is about maximising word "real estate" — every word earns its place. It is NOT about being brief.

**Key distinctions:**
- A 1,500-word memo can be concise if every paragraph advances the argument
- A 150-word message can be meandering if it circles without landing
- Use as many words as necessary — no more, no less
- Never sacrifice clarity or crucial information for brevity

**When to be longer:**
- Complex topics that cannot be simplified without misrepresenting the idea
- When the reader needs context to ask better questions
- When persuasion requires evidence and reasoning

**When to be shorter:**
- Thread replies where the context is already established
- Quick updates where the status is the whole message
- Praise and encouragement (impact comes from brevity)

**Volunteering context:** Don't force readers to extract basic information piece by piece. Share appropriate detail unprompted. Responsible communication anticipates what the reader needs.

## Principle 4: Sales Before Logistics

Any message requesting action needs two components, in this order:
1. **Sales** — generate buy-in and enthusiasm (the "why")
2. **Logistics** — share operational details (the "how")

**The critical error:** Treating a sales problem as a logistics problem. Adding more steps, instructions, or process detail fails when the reader isn't convinced the effort is worthwhile.

**Three common blockers to address:**
- Unconvinced the initiative matters
- Unclear on priority relative to other demands
- Doubtful the effort justifies the outcome

**Choosing the right "why":**
- Connect to recipient self-interest (what benefits them or their team)
- Illustrate pain points avoided or gains achieved
- Ground in logic, not sentiment

**Bad:** "I'd like to get input on this deck" (me-focused, frames as favour)
**Good:** "This is your chance to highlight your team's wins and bring visibility to the work driving results" (them-focused, frames as opportunity)

**Rule:** If uncertain about recipient enthusiasm, lead with benefits. Over-selling when already agreed costs almost nothing. Under-selling when unconvinced guarantees resistance.

## Principle 5: Signposting at Three Levels

Signposting uses words, phrases, and structure to signal what comes next. It dramatically reduces cognitive load — readers always know where they are and where you're taking them.

### Level 1: Document Structure (Memo Level)
- Headers and subheaders
- White space between sections
- Bullet points and numbered lists for parallel items
- Bold for key terms and decisions

### Level 2: Paragraph Structure
- Topic sentences that preview the paragraph's content
- One idea per paragraph
- Transition sentences between sections

### Level 3: Sentence-Level Transitions

Use these phrases to show logical flow:

| Phrase | What it signals |
|--------|----------------|
| **For example** | Illustration coming — skip if you don't need it |
| **Because** | Rationale follows — powerful for persuasion |
| **First, second, third** | Sequential hierarchy through prose |
| **For context** | Background coming — skip if you already know |
| **This means** | Elaboration or implication of what was just said |
| **To be clear** | Proactively addressing a likely objection or misunderstanding |
| **As a next step** | Action item follows — pay attention |
| **Up until now / Going forward** | Marks a transition point — humans notice change |
| **And / But / Therefore / So** | Reveals the logical argument structure |
| **At this point** | Brings narrative to the present — heightens relevance |

**Complexity rule:** Simple ideas need less signposting. Complex or lengthy messages need more. When in doubt, add signposting — it makes long writing feel shorter and faster-paced.

## Principle 6: Calibrate Confidence to Evidence

Match language intensity to your actual certainty and the stakes of being wrong.

**Two extremes to avoid:**
1. **Overconfident assertions** — "This will definitely increase engagement by 40%" (when based on limited data)
2. **Excessive hedging** — "I maybe sort of think we might possibly want to perhaps consider..." (when you have strong evidence)

**The rule:** When you claim certainty sparingly, your confident statements carry greater weight.

### Language Calibration Guide

**High certainty (strong evidence, low cost of being wrong):**
- "We should...", "This is the approach", "The data shows..."

**Moderate certainty (reasonable evidence, some unknowns):**
- "Based on [evidence], we expect...", "My strong recommendation is...", "The most likely outcome..."

**Exploratory (early thinking, seeking input):**
- "I'm working through ideas — my initial hunch is...", "We might try...", "One approach could be..."
- "I'm not stuck on these — where do you disagree?"

**Softening vocabulary (use when appropriate):**
consider, might, could, suggest, seem, probably, likely, in general, try, one way to, in my experience, my initial reaction

**Strengthening vocabulary (use selectively):**
should, must, the way forward, clearly, certainly

### Feedback-Specific Calibration

When the message gives feedback or addresses sensitive topics:
- **Target behaviour, not character:** "I've noticed you tend to..." NOT "You are..."
- **Acknowledge perspective:** "From my point of view..." leaves room for dialogue
- **Observational language:** "It seems like..." rather than "You always..."

### Prescriptive vs Exploratory Framing

**Prescriptive** (discourages pushback): "We should do X, Y, and Z. What do you think?"
**Exploratory** (invites participation): "We might try X, Y, Z. I'm not stuck on these. Where do you disagree?"

Use exploratory framing when you want divergent thinking. Use prescriptive framing when the decision is made and you're communicating direction.

## Principle 7: Format-Specific Rules

### Slack Messages
- Ultra-scannable: key info visible without clicking "Show more"
- Action-oriented: clear asks and next steps
- Thread-conscious: main message is the summary, detail goes in thread
- Notification-aware: @mentions and @channel only when action is needed
- Formatted: strategic bold, bullets, and line breaks
- Assume mobile reading — shorter is almost always better

### Emails
- Subject line: specific, actionable, indicates what's needed ("Decision Needed: ..." not "Quick question")
- Skimmable: key points visible in preview pane
- Appropriate length: detailed enough but respecting inbox time
- Recipients: strategic To/CC/BCC (To = action needed, CC = awareness)
- Consider forwarding: emails get shared — make them self-contained

## Review Checklist

When reviewing a draft, evaluate against these questions:

**Structure:**
- [ ] Main point in the first 2-3 sentences?
- [ ] Context below the point, not above?
- [ ] Signposting present (headers, transitions, bullets)?

**Persuasion:**
- [ ] "Why this matters to the reader" stated (not just "why it matters to the sender")?
- [ ] Sales before logistics (if requesting action)?
- [ ] Benefits concrete and specific?

**Clarity:**
- [ ] Every paragraph advances the message?
- [ ] No chronological storytelling burying the point?
- [ ] Jargon-free (or jargon explained)?
- [ ] One idea per paragraph?

**Tone:**
- [ ] Confidence calibrated to evidence?
- [ ] Exploratory vs prescriptive framing appropriate for the situation?
- [ ] Feedback uses observational language?

**Format:**
- [ ] Appropriate length for channel (Slack: under 300 words, email: varies)?
- [ ] Formatted for skimming (bold, bullets, whitespace)?
- [ ] Clear action items with owners and deadlines?

## Output Format

When reviewing a draft, provide:
1. **Verdict:** Strong / Needs work / Major revision needed
2. **Top 2-3 improvements** (ranked by impact, not exhaustive)
3. **Specific rewrites** for the most impactful changes (show, don't just tell)
4. **What's already working** (1-2 things to keep)

Keep feedback concise. The draft agent should incorporate the most impactful suggestions while maintaining the user's voice. Don't turn every draft into a rewrite exercise — sometimes a message just needs one structural fix.
